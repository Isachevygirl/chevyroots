I'm going to say something that might cost me followers.

**The Camaro is the better car. The Mustang is the better brand.**

Those aren't the same thing. And that difference is exactly why Chevy killed one and Ford didn't.

## The driving case

I'll start with the part that's measurable, because I know some of you are already typing angry comments about resale value and I need you to sit with the driving data first.

The 6th-generation Camaro rides on GM's Alpha platform — the same architecture under the Cadillac ATS and CT4. It's about 300 pounds lighter than a comparable Mustang. The SS 1LE and ZL1 1LE versions held road-course records at multiple tracks, including putting down Nürburgring times that made European sports car owners deeply uncomfortable.

The Mustang rides on Ford's D2C platform, which is heavier, longer, and tuned more for straight-line drama than corner-entry precision. The GT Performance Pack is excellent. The Shelby GT500 is a straight-line weapon. But on a road course — a tight, technical road course where weight transfer and chassis composure matter more than peak power — the Camaro wins.

This isn't my opinion. This is the lap times. The Camaro SS 1LE is faster than the Mustang GT Performance Pack 2 at every track that both cars have been tested on by the same publication. The ZL1 1LE was faster than the GT500 on a road course in multiple head-to-head tests. The Camaro is the sharper instrument.

<Pullquote>The Camaro is what happens when GM's best chassis engineers get carte blanche and a reasonable budget. The result is a car that embarrasses things twice its price on a race track.</Pullquote>

## The brand case

Now here's where Ford wins, and it's not even close.

The Mustang is alive. The Camaro is dead.

Production of the 6th-gen Camaro ended in February 2024. Ford launched the S650 Mustang the same year with the Dark Horse, a manual-only performance trim that exists specifically to serve the track-day crowd that GM abandoned. Ford is also building the $300K GTD, a Le Mans-derived supercar Mustang that competes with Porsche and Ferrari. The Mustang pipeline is deeper, more active, and more visible than the Camaro ever was.

How did this happen? The Camaro outsold the Mustang exactly zero times during their concurrent generation. The Mustang outsold the Camaro by a factor of 2–3x in most years. Ford sold over 75,000 Mustangs in 2023. Chevy sold about 24,000 Camaros. When GM looked at those numbers and looked at the investment required for a 7th-generation platform, they killed the car.

**Ford won the brand war by making the Mustang accessible.** The EcoBoost Mustang starts under $33K and attracts first-time buyers who will eventually upgrade to a GT or a Dark Horse. The Mustang convertible exists in a way the Camaro convertible never thrived. The Mustang has a huge aftermarket, a massive cultural presence in movies and TV, and the name recognition that comes from being continuously produced for 60+ years.

The Camaro had none of that. It was niche, intimidating, expensive at the base level, impossible to see out of, and — this is the part nobody at GM wants to admit — it looked mean in a way that scared off casual buyers. The Camaro attracted enthusiasts. The Mustang attracted *everyone*.

## The visibility problem

Speaking of not being able to see: **the Camaro has the worst rearward visibility of any production car sold in America during the 2016–2024 period.** This isn't hyperbole. The gun-slit rear window, the high beltline, and the thick C-pillars combine to create a rear view that is functionally useless without the rearview camera.

I rented a Camaro SS last month and wrote about it. I loved the driving experience. I also said "visibility out the back is basically a joke." That review was honest. The car is phenomenal to drive and miserable to park.

The Mustang has better visibility. The Mustang is easier to daily drive. The Mustang is more comfortable on long highway drives. The Mustang's interior is nicer in the current generation. Ford invested in making the car livable. GM invested in making the car fast. Both succeeded. But only one of those investments sells 75,000 units a year.

## Why I'm still a Camaro person

I grew up around Camaros. My cousin had a 3rd-gen IROC-Z that he'd take me for rides in when I was twelve. I learned what a V8 sounds like from that car. I have a visceral, identity-level attachment to the Camaro that the Mustang will never touch — not because the Mustang isn't good, but because the Mustang isn't *mine*.

That's what this debate is actually about, underneath all the lap times and sales figures. The Camaro-vs-Mustang debate is a proxy for tribal identity. Ford people and Chevy people have been going at this since before either of us were born. The cars are the surface. The loyalty is the foundation.

<CrystalTake>
Here's the honest take: **if someone asks me which car to buy and they're cross-shopping a Camaro SS and a Mustang GT, I tell them to buy the Mustang.** Not because the Mustang is a better car on a dyno or a skid pad. It isn't. But because the Mustang is easier to live with, easier to sell later, and is going to be supported with parts, warranty work, and future model years in a way the Camaro won't be.

If someone asks me which car I'd rather *drive* for one day on a twisty mountain road — the answer is the Camaro, every time, and it's not close.

**The Camaro is the better car. The Mustang is the better purchase.** If you can find a clean, low-mileage ZL1 1LE before the market realizes what GM killed, buy it. In ten years it'll be the car everyone wishes they'd picked up when it was still affordable. The Mustang will still be in production. The Camaro won't.
</CrystalTake>

## The future

There are rumors of a 7th-generation Camaro coming back, possibly as an EV, possibly as a hybrid. I'll believe it when I see it on a dealer lot. GM has broken Camaro promises before.

If it does come back, I hope they keep what made the 6th gen great — the chassis, the weight balance, the track DNA — and fix what killed it: the visibility, the interior quality, the pricing, and the arrogance of thinking enthusiasts alone could sustain a 75,000-unit-a-year production line.

The Mustang won the sales war. The Camaro won the driving war. History will remember both. But only one of them is still being built, and that matters more than any lap time.
