People ask me why I'm so loyal to Chevy. I get it as a good-natured ribbing from Ford friends, and I get it as an actual question from people who are trying to understand the car culture I grew up in.

The honest answer is: I grew up in Houston, and in Houston, Chevy isn't a brand preference. It's the water you drink and the air you breathe.

Let me explain.

## Houston as the Chevy capital

I don't think people outside Texas understand how deep Chevy runs in Houston specifically. Not Texas generally — Texas is a truck state, and trucks are more fragmented than you'd think (a lot of F-150s, a lot of Rams, a good number of Toyota Tundras). I'm talking about Houston specifically. Houston is a Chevy city.

The Silverado outsells everything else in the Houston metro. Not by a little — by a lot. The dealership density for Chevy in the greater Houston area is wild: twenty-plus dealerships inside Beltway 8, more in the outer rings, and at least three of the biggest Chevy dealers in the country by volume are Houston-area stores. If you live anywhere in Houston, you're within a 15-minute drive of a Chevy dealership, and the one you pass the most often is probably the one your family has been going to for three generations.

When I was little, the Silverados parked outside our neighbors' houses weren't decisions. They were just what Silverados do — they were the shape of Dad's truck. Mom drove an old Impala with a ribbon of chrome down the side. My uncle had a Tahoe. My cousin's first car was a hand-me-down Malibu with 180,000 miles on it that ran forever. Nobody in my family ever drove anything that wasn't a Chevy, and I didn't think that was weird until I moved somewhere else and realized it was.

## The Impala thing

If you know Houston, you know the Impala thing.

The fourth-generation Impala — specifically the '64, the '75, the '85 — is everywhere in Houston in a way it isn't in any other American city. Those cars are part of the Houston aesthetic the same way the skyline and the oil refineries and the slow-rolled traffic on 610 are. You see them at every cruise-in, on every cover of every Houston rap album, at every Friday night high school football game, at every quinceañera.

And the reason is **swangas.**

<Pullquote>Swangas — those extended 30-spoke wheels — were invented in Houston, on Impalas, in the early '80s. They're a Houston thing. If you see a Chevy on swangas outside Houston, that person is either from Houston, knows someone from Houston, or is cosplaying Houston.</Pullquote>

The Impala on swangas, moving slowly down the boulevard with the chopped and screwed hip-hop out the windows — that's the foundational Houston car image. Not the truck. The Impala. And because that aesthetic spread from Houston to everywhere, anyone who touches Houston car culture at any point becomes a Chevy person by osmosis.

I didn't grow up in that specific scene. My family was too suburban for it. But you can't grow up in Houston and not absorb it. It's on every billboard, it's on every music video, it's in the parking lot at every event, and it's the reason the used Impala market in Houston is the most expensive used Impala market in America.

## The Silverado as a rite of passage

The other half of the Houston Chevy story is the Silverado, and that one is simpler: **if you're a working adult in Houston, you have a Silverado.**

Not because you work construction (though half the construction in the city runs on Silverados). Not because you haul trailers (though most of the boat-towing at Lake Conroe and Sam Rayburn is Silverados). You have a Silverado because it's the truck your dad had, and his dad had, and when you got your first real job you walked into the same Chevy dealership your family has used for thirty years and they put you in the newest version of the truck you grew up in.

It's not a loyalty purchase in the sense of "I researched every option and chose Chevy." It's a loyalty purchase in the sense of "this is the truck I trust because my people trusted it first." Those are different things. The first is market analysis. The second is identity.

My first truck was a used '09 Silverado LT that my dad helped me negotiate on when I was 22. I drove it for seven years. I put 140,000 miles on it in Houston traffic. I took it to the beach a dozen times and to Austin for shows and to Dallas for football games. It never left me stranded. When I finally sold it — to a friend of the family, for more than I paid, because Silverados in Houston hold their value like nothing else — the guy who bought it from me still sends me texts about how it's doing.

That's the Silverado in Houston. It's not a vehicle. It's a relationship.

## The Galveston detail

One more thing that non-Houston people don't know: **salt-water damage is real, but in Houston, the Chevys survive it better than anything else.**

Galveston is an hour from Houston. Half the people I know go to Galveston at least twice a year. The bay water is corrosive, the salt spray is constant, and every vehicle that spends time there picks up rust faster than it would in the city.

Houston Chevys — Silverados especially, but also the old Tahoes and the Suburbans — hold up to that salt environment notably better than Ford trucks from the same era. I don't know if it's the paint, the body protection, the undercoating, or something in the specific Texas-assembled Silverados that are built at the Arlington plant. I just know that I've seen twenty-year-old Galveston-coast Silverados that still have clean body panels and solid frames, and I've seen ten-year-old Fords from the same area that look like they've been dissolving in slow motion.

Houston people notice this. Houston people talk about this. When your friend's dad picks you up at the airport in a fifteen-year-old Silverado that looks five years old and the F-150 his neighbor drives is visibly rotting, that conversation shapes your brand loyalty for the rest of your life.

## Moving away

I moved to Virginia a few years ago and the hardest adjustment wasn't the weather or the traffic or the fact that nobody understands what a kolache is. It was the fact that nobody in Virginia cares about Chevys the way Houstonians do.

Northern Virginia is a Mustang town. A lot of it is a Jeep town. The truck guys around here are more likely to drive F-150s than Silverados. The car shows I go to locally are mixed-make in a way Houston shows never are — I show up to 610 Cruisers and at least half the cars aren't Chevys. That still feels wrong to me after two years of living here. In Houston, every cruise-in is 70% Chevy, minimum. It's the default. In Virginia, the default is "whatever someone's driving this weekend."

I'm not complaining. The variety is great. I just want people to understand that when I say I'm a Chevy girl, it's not a decision I made at 22 when I got my first truck. It's a decision the city of Houston made for me by the time I was old enough to know what cars were.

## What this means for ChevyRoots

The site you're reading right now exists because the way Houston relates to Chevy — that deep, familial, multi-generational thing — doesn't exist in the content space online. There are Chevy enthusiast sites and there are Houston culture sites, but there isn't a place where both things are treated like they're the same story. Which they are, for a lot of us.

I'm not going to pretend ChevyRoots is going to be a Houston-first site forever. It's going to cover everything Chevy. But the editorial voice is going to keep that Houston perspective because that's where my perspective comes from, and I think that's a useful thing to bring to a market full of general-automotive content that doesn't have a point of view about anything.

If you're a Houston Chevy person reading this: you know what I'm talking about. If you're not: now maybe you understand a little more about why I drive a bowtie and why I'm building a whole site about it.
