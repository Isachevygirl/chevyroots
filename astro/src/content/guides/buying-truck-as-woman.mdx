<h2 id="the-reality">Let's Be Honest About the Reality</h2>
      <p>I've bought trucks. I've walked into dealerships alone, in work clothes, knowing exactly what I wanted — and I've been asked if my husband was coming. I've been steered toward the Equinox before I even got a word in. I've had salespeople explain to me what a tow rating is after I'd already quoted the spec sheet back at them.</p>
      <p>I'm not here to tell you dealerships are all bad. Some are genuinely great. But I'd be doing you a disservice if I pretended the experience of buying a truck as a woman is the same as buying one as a man. It isn't. And the best thing I can do is prepare you for what you might actually face — and how to walk through it like you own the room.</p>
      <p>Because here's the truth: <strong>you know what you want. You've done your research. You have the money. Nobody in that dealership has more power in this transaction than you do.</strong> The only way they win is if you forget that.</p>

      <div class="pullquote">
        <p>"The only way they win is if you forget who has the power in this transaction."</p>
      </div>

      <h2 id="what-to-expect">What You Might Encounter</h2>
      <p>Not every dealership will do these things. Some salespeople are fantastic. But these are real patterns that women report regularly, and knowing they exist means you won't be caught off guard.</p>

      <h3>Being Steered Somewhere You Didn't Ask to Go</h3>
      <p>You say "I'm looking at the Silverado LTZ." They say "Have you seen the new Equinox? It's really popular with women." This happens. It's insulting. The correct response is simple and calm: <em>"I'm only looking at the Silverado today. Can we go to those?"</em> You don't owe them an explanation. You don't have to justify why you want a truck.</p>

      <h3>"Is Your Husband Coming?"</h3>
      <p>The question implies you're not the decision-maker. My answer: "No. I make my own vehicle purchases." Short. Not angry. Just a fact. Some dealers will pivot immediately and respect that. Others won't — and that tells you everything you need to know about whether you want to give them your money.</p>

      <h3>Explaining Things You Already Know</h3>
      <p>Getting mansplained about towing, payload, or how a 4WD system works when you didn't ask is common. My move: I answer the question they're explaining to me before they finish the sentence. It signals immediately that I already know the material. Most salespeople recalibrate fast once they realize you're informed.</p>

      <h3>The Pressure Tactics</h3>
      <p>These aren't unique to women — everyone gets them. "This deal is only good today." "We have three other buyers looking at this truck." "I can't guarantee the price after today." These are almost always false urgency tactics. A good deal today will be a good deal tomorrow. If they pull the offer because you want to sleep on it, let them. There are hundreds of Silverados available.</p>

<CrystalTake>

The finance manager came out and said the offer was expiring at the end of the day and I'd lose $2,000 in rebates. I said, "Let me get my keys." I walked out. They called me that night with a better number. <strong>The deal almost never actually disappears.</strong> They want to sell you a truck. They just want to sell it at maximum profit. Your job is to find the floor.</p>

      <h2 id="how-to-prepare">How to Prepare Before You Go</h2>
      <p>Preparation is the great equalizer. When you walk in knowing more about the truck than the salesperson, the dynamic shifts. Here's what to do before you set foot on any lot:</p>

      <h3>Know the Truck Completely</h3>
      <ul>
        <li>Look up the MSRP, invoice price, and any current GM incentives at <strong>GMIncentives.com</strong> or <strong>Chevy.com</strong></li>
        <li>Know your RPO codes — which engine, which options matter to you (check our <a href="/guides/chevy-rpo-codes/">RPO Code guide</a>)</li>
        <li>Know the tow rating, payload, and bed dimensions if they matter to your purchase</li>
        <li>Know what the truck you want is actually going for at other dealers (use TrueCar, Edmunds, or dealer websites in your region)</li>
      </ul>

      <h3>Get Pre-Approved Before You Go</h3>
      <p>Pre-approval from your bank or credit union does two things: it tells you exactly what you can buy, and it removes the dealer's financing leverage. Dealer financing can be fine — but it's also where dealers make significant profit. If you walk in pre-approved, you're choosing financing on your terms. Tell them you have financing in place but you're willing to hear their numbers.</p>

      <ul class="checklist">
        <li><strong>Get pre-approved</strong> — bank, credit union, or online lender (Capital One Auto, LightStream)</li>
        <li><strong>Know your credit score</strong> — don't let them tell you your rate without knowing your baseline</li>
        <li><strong>Set your out-the-door budget</strong> — including tax, title, and dealer fees</li>
        <li><strong>Research trade-in value</strong> separately — don't bundle it into the negotiation</li>
      </ul>

      <h2 id="what-to-say">What to Say: Real Scripts</h2>

      <h2 id="finding-good-dealers">How to Find Dealers Who Respect You</h2>
      <p>Not all dealers are equal. Here's how to find the good ones before you waste a Saturday:</p>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>Google reviews, filtered by women:</strong> Read the reviews. Women specifically mention when they felt respected or dismissed. This is real data.</li>
        <li><strong>Call before you go:</strong> Ask a single question about the truck you want. How they handle someone on the phone who clearly knows what they're talking about tells you a lot.</li>
        <li><strong>Ask for a different salesperson:</strong> If your first interaction is dismissive, you're not obligated to continue with that person. "Could I work with someone else?" is a completely normal request.</li>
        <li><strong>Women-run dealerships:</strong> They exist. A quick search in your area will surface female GMs and sales directors. Not a guarantee of experience, but worth knowing.</li>
      </ul>

      <h2 id="online-buying">The Online Option Is Real Now</h2>
      <p>If the traditional dealership experience isn't worth the energy to you, that's a completely valid stance. The online buying landscape has gotten genuinely good:</p>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>Carvana / Vroom:</strong> No-haggle used trucks with home delivery. No salespeople. I've used Carvana and had a smooth experience. The prices aren't always the best, but the process is controlled.</li>
        <li><strong>GM's Online Shop:</strong> Some Chevy dealers now offer a fully online purchase process where you configure, price, and finance without setting foot in a showroom until pickup.</li>
        <li><strong>Out-of-state dealers:</strong> Especially for new trucks, calling dealers in a larger metro 2–3 hours away can yield significantly better pricing. Many will deliver. I've bought vehicles this way.</li>
      </ul>

<TipBox label="The F&I Office">
<p>The Finance & Insurance office at the end of the deal is where many people lose hundreds to thousands of dollars on extended warranties, paint protection, and gap insurance they don't need at the price offered. Every single item in the F&I menu is negotiable or declinable. "No thank you" is a complete sentence. Extended warranties specifically: compare prices through third-party providers or directly through GM after the sale — they're almost always cheaper than the F&I desk rate.</p>
</TipBox>

      <h2 id="the-walk">The Walk-Away Is Your Greatest Weapon</h2>
      <p>There are 4,000+ Chevy dealers in the United States. Every single Silverado LTZ with a 6.2L and the NHT package is not the last one in existence. <strong>You can always walk away.</strong> I mean this literally and practically: standing up, thanking them for their time, and leaving is the single most powerful move you can make in any dealership negotiation.</p>
      <p>When you walk away calmly, without anger, leaving your contact information — you've communicated that you're a serious buyer who has options. In most cases, if there's any room on the deal, you'll get a call within 24 hours. Not always. But often enough that it's always worth doing if you can't reach a number that works for you.</p>

<CrystalTake>
<p>I once stayed too long at a dealership, got tired, and agreed to $1,200 more than I should have just to be done with it. I was exhausted and emotionally done with the process. I drove home fine, but I thought about that money for a long time. <strong>Now I have a hard stop: if I've been there more than 90 minutes and we're still not at a number I'm happy with, I leave.</strong> Being tired is not a negotiating strategy. Leaving and coming back fresh, or going to the next dealer, almost always produces a better result than grinding it out past your limit.</p>
        <p>You deserve the truck you want, at a price that's fair, from people who treat you like the capable adult you are. Don't settle for less because someone in a showroom tried to make you feel like you should.</p>
