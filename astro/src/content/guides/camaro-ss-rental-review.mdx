Let me set the scene. It's a Tuesday. I show up to the rental counter fully expecting to walk out with a beige Nissan Altima and go on with my life. Instead, the guy behind the desk says they're out of midsize sedans and asks if I'd be okay with — and I quote — "something a little sportier." Reader, it was a 2024 Camaro SS in **Rapid Blue**. I said yes before he finished the sentence.

This isn't a review from a press loan. Nobody gave me talking points. I didn't sign a media embargo. I rented a car, it happened to be a muscle car, and I spent the next 10 hours finding out what it's like when the thing you've always heard about is suddenly in your hands.

## First Impression: It's Low

First impression the second you lay eyes on it in the lot: it's low. Like, *actually* low. Squatted, wide-shouldered, aggressive in a way that reads less "sports car" and more "I will absolutely do something your insurance doesn't cover." The Rapid Blue popped in the morning light — this deep metallic blue that shifts almost purple depending on the angle. I stood next to it for probably two minutes before I even got in.

The design language of the 6th-gen Camaro is polarizing and I know that. People either think it looks mean or they think it looks angry in a way that's trying too hard. I'm in the first camp. This car has presence. When I parked it at a coffee shop later that morning, two people stopped to look at it. One asked what it was. Nobody asks what a Mustang is. People ask what a Camaro is. That tells you something about who these cars attract.

## Starting It

Then I started it. Oh. The V8 fires up with this deep, rumbly exhale — not a scream, not a bark, just a confident growl that says *I've been waiting*. Pulling out of the lot, I kept the windows down even though it was a little chilly because I needed to hear every single thing going on under that hood.

The 6.2L LT1 V8 produces 455 horsepower and 455 lb-ft of torque. In a car that weighs about 3,700 pounds. The power-to-weight ratio is real, and you feel it the first time you merge onto a highway. The 10-speed automatic finds the right gear almost instantly, and the throttle response in Sport mode is sharper than anything I've driven short of the ZR2's 6.2 in a much lighter package.

<Pullquote>First corner at normal speed and the steering already feels connected and direct in a way my daily just doesn't. It's communicating with you. You can feel the road.</Pullquote>

## The Best 10 Seconds of the Day

The best moment of the day came somewhere on a back highway with no one around. I got on it. Not stupid-fast, just — let it breathe. The exhaust opened up, the whole car flattened out, and for about ten seconds I completely understood why people build their lives around these things. It wasn't just speed. It was the whole experience: the sound, the pull, the way the seat is holding you while everything around you starts moving faster. That's the Camaro. It's theater and it's performance at the same time.

I've driven fast SUVs and fast trucks. The ZR2 is quick. A tuned Tahoe can surprise you. But **a Camaro SS on an open road with the windows down is a different category of experience.** It's not about the number on the speedometer. It's about the way the whole car conspires to make you feel like you're doing something important, even when you're just driving to lunch.

## The Interior

The interior is fine — nothing fancy, comfortable enough, the 8-inch infotainment works without fighting you, the heated seats came in handy on a chilly morning, and the gauge cluster has that "let me show you what the engine is doing" design that GM does well. The steering wheel is chunky in the right way. The shift paddles are responsive.

But visibility. Oh, the visibility.

## The Visibility Problem

I need to say this clearly because the internet tends to gloss over it: **you cannot see out the back of this car.** The rearview mirror shows you a sliver of sky and a thin band of whatever's behind you. The C-pillars are massive. The rear window is a mail slot. Lane changes require the blind-spot monitor or an act of faith.

I spent the first hour adjusting my mirrors and my expectations. By the afternoon I'd adapted — you just use the cameras and the mirrors and accept that the rear window is decorative. But it's genuinely the worst rearward visibility I've experienced in any car, and for first-timers it will be startling.

The rear seat exists mostly as a conversation piece. An adult human can technically sit back there. They will not enjoy it. Use it for jackets, a gym bag, or a small dog that doesn't mind the noise.

<TipBox label="Practical Note">
If you're considering buying a Camaro as a daily driver, test the visibility before committing. Spend 30 minutes in real traffic — parking, lane changes, backing out of a tight spot. If it's fine for you, great. If it bothers you after 30 minutes, it'll bother you every day for 5 years.
</TipBox>

## What Surprised Me

**The ride quality.** I expected the SS to ride like a go-kart. It doesn't. The Magnetic Ride Control (on this particular SS, optioned with it) irons out bad pavement in a way I genuinely didn't expect. The car is firm — it's a sports car — but it's not punishing. I drove it for ten hours and my back didn't hurt at the end.

**The fuel economy.** I averaged 22 mpg over the day, mostly highway. The LT1 has cylinder deactivation (Active Fuel Management) that drops to 4 cylinders at cruise. On the highway at 65 mph, the V8 is barely working. I expected 15 mpg and got much better.

**The attention.** Two people stopped to talk to me about the car at a coffee shop. One kid on a bicycle yelled "nice car!" at a red light. An older gentleman at a gas station told me he had a '69 Camaro and asked if I wanted to see a photo. (I did. It was beautiful.) This car attracts a specific kind of positive attention that trucks don't.

## What I'd Change

**The exhaust sound in Normal mode is too quiet.** I understand why — noise regulations, neighbor friendliness, all that — but the difference between Normal and Sport mode is dramatic enough that you wonder why they bothered with the quiet setting. If I owned this car, I'd leave it in Sport mode permanently.

**The trunk is small.** I knew this going in, but for a car this size, the trunk is phone-booth-level. You can fit one rolling suitcase and a soft bag. A weekend trip for two people is tight. A trip with golf clubs is not happening.

**No manual transmission on this one.** The rental was the 10-speed automatic. I'm told the manual Camaro SS is a significantly different experience — more involvement, better feel, the satisfaction of rowing your own gears. I'll make a point to drive a manual Camaro before production ends. (Update: production has ended. This makes me sad.)

## The Verdict

Would I rent it again? **Yes. Immediately. Without thinking about it.** Would I want to own one? That's a conversation for another day, but I'll say this — it made my daily feel very boring on the drive back. That's the Camaro effect. It recalibrates you.

<CrystalTake>
The 6th-gen Camaro SS is the kind of car that GM should be embarrassed they killed. It's the best-handling American muscle car ever built, the V8 sounds exactly right, and the driving experience is transcendent in a way that the numbers can't capture.

But I understand why they killed it. It doesn't sell. Visibility is bad. The interior can't compete with the Mustang. The base price creeps toward $40K when you add the options that actually make it great. Enthusiasts love it. The market didn't.

**My rating: 8.5/10.** Would rent again. Probably won't own one because I'm a truck girl. But if you can find a clean SS or ZL1 1LE on the used market right now, before the collectors notice — do it. This car is going to appreciate.
</CrystalTake>
