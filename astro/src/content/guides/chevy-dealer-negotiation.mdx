<p>Buying a new Chevy is one of the largest purchases most people will make — and most people walk into it completely unprepared. Dealers negotiate vehicles for a living. You do it once every few years. That information and experience gap is real, and it costs buyers thousands of dollars they didn't have to spend.</p>

      <p>This guide closes that gap. Whether you're buying a Silverado, Tahoe, Equinox, or anything else wearing a bowtie, the strategy is the same.</p>

      <h2 id="research">Step 1: Know the Numbers Before You Go</h2>

      <p>The single most important thing you can do before visiting a dealer is understand the difference between <strong>MSRP and invoice price</strong>.</p>

      <p><strong>MSRP</strong> (Manufacturer's Suggested Retail Price) is what's on the window sticker. It's the dealer's opening ask. <strong>Invoice price</strong> is what the dealer paid GM for the vehicle. The gap between these two numbers is your negotiating room — and on a Silverado or Tahoe, that gap can be $3,000–$6,000 or more depending on the trim and market conditions.</p>

      <ul>
        <li><strong>Edmunds True Market Value (TMV)</strong> shows you what people are actually paying in your zip code — not the sticker, not the invoice, but real transaction prices.</li>
        <li><strong>Holdback:</strong> Dealers also receive a holdback from GM — typically 2–3% of MSRP — after the sale. This exists even when they claim to be selling at or near invoice. Factor it in.</li>
        <li><strong>Market Adjustment:</strong> If a dealer has added a "market adjustment" to the sticker, that's entirely made up. It has no basis in the vehicle's value. Push back immediately or walk.</li>
      </ul>

      <h2 id="timing">Step 2: Time Your Purchase Right</h2>

      <p>When you buy matters almost as much as how you negotiate. Dealers have monthly and quarterly sales targets, and when they're close to hitting a tier, they're far more willing to deal.</p>

      <h3>Best Times to Buy a New Chevy</h3>

      <ul>
        <li><strong>End of the month</strong> — Sales managers are watching the board. If they need one more unit to hit their number, you have leverage.</li>
        <li><strong>End of quarter: March, June, September, December</strong> — Quarterly targets mean amplified pressure. December is typically the single best month of the year.</li>
        <li><strong>End of model year (August–October)</strong> — Dealers need to move outgoing inventory to make room for the new year. Discounts on "previous year" vehicles can be substantial even though the trucks are brand new and still under full factory warranty.</li>
        <li><strong>Weekdays</strong> — Less foot traffic means more attention from the sales team and more flexibility to negotiate without someone else waiting to pay MSRP.</li>
      </ul>

<TipBox label="Timing Tip">
<p>Show up on the last Saturday of December around 3 PM. It's the single highest-pressure selling environment of the year. Dealers will deal.</p>
</TipBox>

      <h2 id="incentives">Step 3: Stack GM Incentives</h2>

      <p>GM offers multiple rebate and incentive programs that can be stacked for serious savings. These are in addition to dealer discounts — they're not substitutes.</p>

      <h3>GM Incentive Programs to Know</h3>

      <ul>
        <li><strong>Cash Rebates:</strong> GM frequently offers $2,000–$5,000 cash back on slow-moving trims or during promotional periods. Check <a href="https://www.chevrolet.com/upcoming-vehicles" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">Chevrolet.com</a> and GM's current offers before you go.</li>
        <li><strong>Loyalty Discount:</strong> If you currently own or lease a GM vehicle, you may qualify for an additional $500–$1,500 loyalty bonus.</li>
        <li><strong>Conquest Bonus:</strong> If you're coming from a non-GM brand, GM often runs conquest offers to steal market share. Ask your dealer specifically about conquest eligibility.</li>
        <li><strong>0% Financing Offers:</strong> These often come with lower rebates. Run the math both ways — sometimes the cash is worth more than the zero-percent rate depending on your financing amount and term.</li>
      </ul>

      <h2 id="supplier">Step 4: Know About Supplier and Employee Pricing</h2>

      <p><strong>GM Supplier Pricing</strong> is available to employees of companies that supply parts or services to General Motors. If you or someone in your household works for a GM supplier, this pricing can be significant — typically 1–5% below invoice, with no negotiation required.</p>

      <p><strong>GM Employee Pricing</strong> is available to GM employees and their families. If you know someone at GM, it's worth asking.</p>

      <p>Even without these programs, you can sometimes find dealers advertising "employee pricing events" — these are sales promotions that use similar language but aren't the same as the real program. Ask specifically: "Is this the GM Supplier Price Program?" If not, it's marketing.</p>

      <h2 id="walkaway">Step 5: The Walk-Away Strategy</h2>

      <p>The single most powerful tool in your negotiation is your willingness to leave. Dealers know that once you walk out the door, your emotional investment resets. They would rather close the deal than have you shop elsewhere.</p>

      <ol class="step-list">
        <li><strong>Make one offer below your target price.</strong> Start 8–12% below MSRP, factoring in any cash rebates separately.</li>
        <li><strong>Wait in silence.</strong> Don't fill the silence. The first person to speak gives ground.</li>
        <li><strong>If they counter high, say:</strong> "That doesn't work for me. Let me know if you get closer to [your number]." Then stand up.</li>
        <li><strong>Leave your contact info.</strong> "I'm shopping three dealers this week. If you hit that number, call me." This is true — you should be shopping multiple dealers.</li>
        <li><strong>Wait 24 hours.</strong> Many deals happen the morning after you walk away when the sales manager calls you back.</li>
      </ol>

      <h2 id="payment">Step 6: Negotiate Price, Not Payment</h2>

      <p>This is where most buyers get taken. When a salesperson asks "what monthly payment are you looking for?", they're shifting the conversation away from the vehicle's actual price toward a number they can manipulate with loan term length.</p>

<WarningBox label="Never Do This">
<p>Never anchor your negotiation to a monthly payment number. A dealer can hit your $650/month target with a 72-month loan at a bad interest rate while charging you thousands more than you should have paid. Always negotiate the <strong>out-the-door price</strong> first.</p>
</WarningBox>

      <p><strong>Out-the-door (OTD) price</strong> means the total you will write a check for, including the vehicle, all taxes, title, registration, and any legitimate fees. This is the number to negotiate. Once it's agreed upon, financing becomes a separate conversation.</p>

      <h2 id="tradein">Step 7: Handle Your Trade-In Separately</h2>

      <p>Dealers use your trade-in as another variable to obscure the real price of the transaction. Get outside offers before you walk in.</p>

      <ul>
        <li><strong>Carmax:</strong> Will give you a written offer good for 7 days. Take 30 minutes and get one.</li>
        <li><strong>Carvana:</strong> Online instant offer. Takes 5 minutes.</li>
        <li><strong>KBB Instant Cash Offer:</strong> Available at many dealers but independent of the negotiation.</li>
      </ul>

      <p>Walk in with your best outside offer in hand and tell the dealer: <strong>"I have a $27,400 Carmax offer on my trade. Beat it or I'll sell it separately."</strong> Now the trade-in is a separate line item, not a negotiation lever.</p>

      <h2 id="fi">Step 8: The F&amp;I Office — What to Say Yes and No To</h2>

      <p>The Finance and Insurance (F&amp;I) office is where dealers make significant back-end profit after you've agreed on the vehicle price. Go in knowing exactly what you'll consider.</p>

      <p>The universal approach: <strong>"I'm not adding anything to the vehicle price today. If I decide I want any of these products, I'll contact you after I've had time to compare third-party pricing."</strong> Say it once, don't argue, repeat it if necessary.</p>

      <h2 id="crystal">Crystal's Take: I'm a Woman Who Buys Trucks. Here's How I Handle It.</h2>

<CrystalTake>
<p>I want to talk about something most buying guides won't: <strong>women are treated differently at dealerships.</strong> This isn't every dealer, and it's not always intentional, but it happens enough that you should be prepared for it.</p>
        <p>I've walked in to buy a truck — knowing exactly what I wanted, what it was worth, and what I was willing to pay — and had salespeople ask if my husband was joining me. I've had the conversation steered toward color options before we got to trim level. I've been quoted higher than sticker when I made the mistake of looking too interested.</p>
        <p>Here's what I do now: <strong>I walk in already knowing the invoice price, the current rebates, and three competing dealer quotes.</strong> I lead with information, not enthusiasm. "I've been offered $X at Dealer Y. I'd prefer to buy here. Can you match it?" That framing removes the emotional negotiation entirely and makes it transactional.</p>
        <p>I also bring documentation — a printed Edmunds TMV report, my Carmax trade-in offer, and the GM rebate page. Paper in front of you changes the dynamic. It signals that you did homework and can't be bulldozed.</p>
        <p><strong>If a dealer doesn't respect that, their lot has trucks I don't need to buy.</strong> There are good dealerships run by people who treat customers fairly regardless of gender. When you find one, they earn your loyalty. The ones who don't earn the referral you give to someone else.</p>
        <p>You deserve to get the same deal as anyone else walking through that door. Know your numbers, stay calm, and be willing to leave. The truck will be there or somewhere else — and that's entirely okay.</p>
</CrystalTake>
