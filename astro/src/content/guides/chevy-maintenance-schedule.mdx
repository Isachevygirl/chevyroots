<p>Modern Chevys are more maintenance-friendly than ever — but "more friendly" doesn't mean "maintenance-free." The Oil Life Monitor system tells you when to change oil. What it doesn't do is remind you about transmission fluid, differential fluid, or spark plugs — the items that cause expensive failures when neglected. This guide covers every major service interval for current Chevy trucks and SUVs, with the honest context your owner's manual buries in fine print.</p>

      <h2 id="oil">Oil Changes: Dexos1 Gen 3 and the OLM</h2>

      <div class="interval-card">
        <div class="interval-card-header">
          <div class="interval-card-name">Engine Oil</div>
          <div class="interval-card-miles">7,500 – 10,000 miles</div>
        </div>
        <div class="interval-card-body">
          Modern Chevy trucks with the 5.3L, 6.2L, or 2.7L turbo are factory-filled with <strong>Dexos1 Gen 3 full synthetic</strong> — GM's licensed spec oil. Do not substitute a non-Dexos1 oil without understanding what you're giving up. The specification matters for the AFM/DFM (cylinder deactivation) system and for GDI engine deposit control. 0W-20 is the spec for most current Silverados and SUVs; verify your specific engine spec in the owner's manual or door jamb sticker. The Oil Life Monitor calculates remaining oil life based on engine temperature cycles, short trips, and operating conditions — it's more accurate than a fixed mileage interval. Follow it. But never go past 10,000 miles or one year, whichever comes first.

      <h2 id="transmission">Transmission Fluid: The 10-Speed Problem</h2>

      <div class="warning-box">
        <div class="warning-box-label">Critical: Read This Before You Skip It</div>
        <p><strong>The Hydra-Matic 10L80 and 10L90 10-speed automatics in Silverado 1500, Tahoe, Suburban, and Camaro are extremely sensitive to transmission fluid degradation.</strong> GM's owner's manual calls the fluid "filled for life" under normal conditions — this is one of the most misunderstood statements in modern vehicle ownership. It does not mean the fluid never degrades. It means GM believes it won't need changing under perfectly ideal driving. Real-world driving is not ideal.</p>
      </div>

      <div class="interval-card urgent">
        <div class="interval-card-header">
          <div class="interval-card-name">Transmission Fluid (10-Speed)</div>
          <div class="interval-card-miles">45,000 – 60,000 miles</div>
        </div>
        <div class="interval-card-body">
          We recommend changing the fluid in any 10-speed Chevy by <strong>45,000–60,000 miles</strong> regardless of what the owner's manual says. The 10-speed's complex clutch pack behavior is sensitive to fluid quality — degraded fluid is a primary contributor to the well-documented transmission shudder complaints that plague high-mileage 10-speed trucks. Use only <strong>DEXRON HP</strong> (the correct spec for the 10L80/10L90) — not DEXRON VI, which is an older spec that does not meet the friction requirements of these transmissions. Many shops still use DEXRON VI because it's cheaper and more available. Make sure yours uses the right fluid. A transmission fluid service runs $150–$300 at a dealer and is cheap insurance against a potential $4,000–$8,000 transmission rebuild.
        </div>
      </div>

      <h2 id="plugs">Spark Plugs: Leave the Iridiums Alone (Until 97,500)</h2>

      <div class="interval-card">
        <div class="interval-card-header">
          <div class="interval-card-name">Spark Plugs</div>
          <div class="interval-card-miles">97,500 miles</div>
        </div>
        <div class="interval-card-body">
          The factory iridium-tip plugs in current Chevy V8s (5.3L, 6.2L) and the 2.7L turbo are rated to <strong>97,500 miles</strong>. Do not replace them early with copper plugs — the iridium tip's fine electrode allows for optimal ignition at the higher compression ratios of modern engines. A misfiring plug before 97,500 miles is usually a sign of something else wrong (oil consumption, a bad coil, injector issue) — don't automatically reach for the plug box first. When you do replace them, use the OE spec iridium plug for your engine. ACDelco, NGK Iridium, and Denso Iridium are all acceptable brands for the service replacement. Labor note: the 6.2L V8's rear bank plugs are a tight reach and may require specialized extensions — factor in extra shop labor or a full day if doing it yourself.
        </div>
      </div>

      <h2 id="coolant">Coolant: Long Life Doesn't Mean Forever</h2>

      <div class="interval-card">
        <div class="interval-card-header">
          <div class="interval-card-name">Engine Coolant (DEXCOOL)</div>
          <div class="interval-card-miles">150,000 miles or 5 years</div>
        </div>
        <div class="interval-card-body">
          GM's DEXCOOL orange coolant is an OAT (Organic Acid Technology) formulation rated for <strong>150,000 miles or 5 years</strong> from new — but the time-based interval matters. If you bought a 4-year-old truck and haven't changed the coolant, change it now regardless of mileage. Degraded DEXCOOL turns acidic and corrodes aluminum components — water pump housing, heater core fittings, and cylinder head gasket surfaces are all at risk. Do not mix DEXCOOL with green conventional coolant; the chemical incompatibility creates a gel-like precipitate that blocks coolant passages. Full flush, distilled water, and fresh DEXCOOL concentrate mixed 50/50 is the correct service.
        </div>
      </div>

      <h2 id="driveline">4WD Driveline Fluids: The Interval Most Owners Miss</h2>

      <p>Transfer case fluid, front differential fluid, and rear differential fluid are all <strong>45,000-mile service items</strong> on 4WD trucks. These are frequently missed because there's no warning light, no Oil Life Monitor, and no reminder. The consequence of neglect is accelerating wear in components that are expensive to replace — a rear differential rebuild on a Silverado runs $1,500–$3,000 at a dealer.</p>

      <h3>Rear Differential: One Extra Step for Limited Slip</h3>
      <p>Silverados with the Eaton G80 Gov-Lock or the available electronic locking rear differential require <strong>friction modifier additive</strong> in the differential fluid — typically 4 oz of GM Limited Slip Additive mixed with the gear lube. Without it, the clutch packs in the limited-slip unit will chatter and wear prematurely. This is easy to overlook if you're having the service done at a non-dealer shop that doesn't check for the limited-slip specification.</p>

      <div class="tip-box">
        <div class="tip-box-label">DIY Tip</div>
        <p>Transfer case fluid and differential fluid changes are straightforward DIY jobs on Silverados with basic tools — a drain plug, fill plug, and a fluid pump. <strong>Motul Gear 300 75W-90</strong> and <strong>ACDelco Autotrack II</strong> for the transfer case are popular OEM-quality choices. Just be certain to fill to the bottom of the fill port, not all the way to the top.</p>
      </div>

      <h2 id="common-mistakes">The Most Common Maintenance Mistakes</h2>

      <ul>
        <li><strong>Treating "filled for life" transmission fluid as literally true.</strong> Change your 10-speed fluid by 45,000–60,000 miles. The service interval exists because real-world driving isn't "normal conditions."</li>
        <li><strong>Using DEXRON VI in the 10L80/10L90.</strong> It is the wrong spec. Use DEXRON HP only in these transmissions.</li>
        <li><strong>Skipping the differential fluid on limited-slip axles.</strong> Forgetting the friction modifier additive on an Eaton G80 will cause chatter and early wear.</li>
        <li><strong>Running non-Dexos1 oil.</strong> Third-party synthetics that haven't earned the GM Dexos1 Gen 3 license may not meet the deposit control requirements for direct-injection and cylinder deactivation engines.</li>
        <li><strong>Ignoring the brake fluid interval.</strong> It absorbs moisture over time, lowering its boiling point. Every 45,000 miles or 3 years is the correct rhythm — especially on tow vehicles where brake temperatures run higher.</li>
        <li><strong>Delaying coolant service on used vehicles.</strong> If you don't have documentation of a coolant change, change it. The cost of a heater core or water pump on a truck with acidic degraded coolant is far higher than a flush.</li>
      </ul>
