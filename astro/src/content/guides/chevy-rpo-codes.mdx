<h2 id="what-are-rpo">What Are RPO Codes?</h2>
      <p><strong>RPO stands for Regular Production Option.</strong> It's GM's internal coding system for every option, package, engine, transmission, and feature that goes into a vehicle. Every Chevy built since the 1970s has an RPO label, and it tells you exactly what that specific truck was built with — not what the trim level typically comes with, but what <em>this truck</em> actually has.</p>
      <p>This matters enormously when buying used. Two trucks can be the same trim level and look identical on the outside while being completely different in capability. The RPO label reveals the truth. It's the difference between a base-spec work truck and a max-tow, locking-diff, off-road machine hiding under the same paint job.</p>

<TipBox label="Where to Find It">
<p>Open the glove box and look for a white label, usually 4–6 inches wide, with rows of three-character alphanumeric codes. On some models it's on the inside of the glove box door itself. On older trucks (pre-2000) it may be in the center console, behind the ashtray, or on the spare tire cover in the bed.</p>
</TipBox>

      <h2 id="how-to-read">How to Read the Label</h2>
      <p>The label lists every RPO code that applies to that vehicle, separated by spaces. They're not alphabetical — they're grouped by system (powertrain, chassis, interior, etc.) but not in any obvious order. You don't need to decode every single one. What you're looking for are the <strong>high-value codes</strong> that significantly affect capability or resale value.</p>
      <p>Some codes are present on every truck (like the paint code, which always appears). Others are only on trucks that were ordered with that specific option. The absence of a code is just as informative as its presence — if G80 isn't there, you don't have a locking rear differential.</p>

      <h2 id="the-codes">The RPO Codes That Actually Matter</h2>
      <p>These are the codes that meaningfully affect what a truck can do, what it's worth, and how it's equipped. Learn these and you'll know more about a used truck than most people selling them.</p>

      <div class="rpo-grid">
        <div class="rpo-card high-value">
          <div class="rpo-code">G80</div>
          <div class="rpo-name">Locking Rear Differential (Eaton)</div>
          <div class="rpo-desc">The Eaton Gov-Lok limited-slip differential. Automatically locks when one wheel loses traction. Essential for off-road, towing in soft terrain, and winter driving. A $500+ option new; look for it on used trucks. Silently significant.</div>
        </div>
        <div class="rpo-card high-value">
          <div class="rpo-code">NHT</div>
          <div class="rpo-name">Max Trailering Package</div>
          <div class="rpo-desc">This is the heavy-duty towing bundle — upgraded rear axle ratio, heavy-duty cooling, trailer brake controller, 7-pin connector, and more. Unlocks the truck's maximum published tow rating. Without NHT, you're leaving 1,000–3,000+ lbs of tow capacity on the table.</div>
        </div>
        <div class="rpo-card high-value">
          <div class="rpo-code">Z71</div>
          <div class="rpo-name">Off-Road Suspension Package</div>
          <div class="rpo-desc">Rancho shock absorbers, skid plates (front differential, transfer case, fuel tank), hill descent control, and locking front differential on 4WD models. This is a genuine capability package, not just a sticker. Also signals the NZZ skid plate group.</div>
        </div>
        <div class="rpo-card high-value">
          <div class="rpo-code">Z82</div>
          <div class="rpo-name">Trailering Package</div>
          <div class="rpo-desc">The standard trailering bundle below NHT. Includes integrated trailer brake controller, 7-pin/4-pin trailer connectors, trailer sway control, and hitch guidance camera support. Required for activating trailer sway control on most models.</div>
        </div>
        <div class="rpo-card">
          <div class="rpo-code">L84</div>
          <div class="rpo-name">5.3L V8 EcoTec3 (355 hp)</div>
          <div class="rpo-desc">The standard V8. 355 horsepower, 383 lb-ft torque. AFM/DFM cylinder deactivation active. The workhorse of the Silverado lineup, found in LT Trail Boss, LTZ, and High Country trims when the 6.2L isn't selected.</div>
        </div>
        <div class="rpo-card">
          <div class="rpo-code">L87</div>
          <div class="rpo-name">6.2L V8 EcoTec3 (420 hp)</div>
          <div class="rpo-desc">The top gas engine. 420 hp, 460 lb-ft. Available only on LTZ and High Country with the premium package. Paired with the 10-speed automatic. Adds significant resale value and towing capability. If you're buying a used LTZ, check for L87 — not all have it.</div>
        </div>
        <div class="rpo-card">
          <div class="rpo-code">LM2</div>
          <div class="rpo-name">3.0L Duramax Inline-6 Diesel</div>
          <div class="rpo-desc">277 hp, 460 lb-ft torque. The smoothest engine in the lineup, with best-in-class diesel fuel economy. Look for LZ0 on newer 2022+ trucks (the refreshed version). Commands a premium on used trucks — expect to pay $2,000–4,000 more over an equivalent gas model.</div>
        </div>
        <div class="rpo-card">
          <div class="rpo-code">MYC</div>
          <div class="rpo-name">10-Speed Automatic Transmission</div>
          <div class="rpo-desc">The Hydra-Matic 10L80 (gas V8) or 10L90 (6.2L). Paired with all V8 engines on current Silverados. Note: the 2.7T gets its own 8-speed (MHU). If you're comparing older trucks, MYC confirms the truck has the modern 10-speed, not the older 8-speed (M5U).</div>
        </div>
        <div class="rpo-card">
          <div class="rpo-code">NZZ</div>
          <div class="rpo-name">Z71 Off-Road Package (Skid Plates)</div>
          <div class="rpo-desc">Usually appears alongside Z71. Specifically refers to the underbody skid plate protection group: front differential, transfer case, and fuel tank. Verify NZZ is present on used Z71 trucks — aftermarket skid plates don't carry this code.</div>
        </div>
      </div>

      <h2 id="buying-used">How to Use RPO Codes When Buying Used</h2>
      <p>When you're looking at a used Silverado, the RPO label is your cheat sheet. Here's how to use it strategically:</p>

      <h3>Before You Go to the Lot</h3>
      <p>Know the codes you want. If you're buying a tow rig, you want <strong>NHT</strong> and <strong>G80</strong>. If you want a capable off-roader, check for <strong>Z71</strong>, <strong>NZZ</strong>, and <strong>G80</strong>. If you specifically want the 6.2L, look for <strong>L87</strong> — don't rely on the seller to know the difference from the 5.3L.</p>

      <h3>At the Truck</h3>
      <p>Open the glove box before you do anything else. Photograph the RPO label. Then cross-reference the codes against what the seller claims the truck has. Discrepancies are common — not always deceptive, often just ignorant — but they affect price.</p>

      <h3>Negotiating with Knowledge</h3>
      <p>A truck without NHT has a lower tow rating, which is a legitimate price difference. A truck with L87 instead of L84 is worth more. A Z71 without G80 is missing a key off-road feature. These gaps give you real, factual leverage at the negotiation table — not just "I found it cheaper elsewhere."</p>

<CrystalTake>
<p>I've personally caught dealers listing trucks as "Max Tow" when they didn't have the NHT package. Not maliciously — they just didn't know. When I pulled the RPO label and pointed to the missing code, the conversation changed immediately. <strong>Knowledge is negotiating power.</strong> Print this page, bring it with you, and don't let anyone tell you a truck has a capability it doesn't have.</p>
</CrystalTake>
