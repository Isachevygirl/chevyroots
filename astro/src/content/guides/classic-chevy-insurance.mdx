<h2 id="why-special">Why Classic Cars Need Special Insurance</h2>
      <p>Your standard auto insurance policy is designed for daily-driven vehicles that depreciate over time. Classic cars are the opposite — they appreciate. A '57 Bel Air in good condition is worth more today than it was five years ago, and far more than it would be under your regular insurer's "actual cash value" calculation, which factors in age and depreciation.</p>
      <p>If a standard insurer totals your classic, they pay current market value minus depreciation. For a car from 1957, that calculation is a disaster. <strong>Classic car insurance solves this with agreed value coverage</strong> — you and the insurer agree on the car's value upfront, and that's what you get paid if it's totaled or stolen. No depreciation. No arguments. No surprises.</p>

<TipBox label="Agreed Value vs. Stated Value vs. Actual Cash Value">
<p><strong>Agreed Value:</strong> You and the insurer agree on a specific value. That exact amount is paid at total loss. Best option for classics. <br/><strong>Stated Value:</strong> You state a value, but the insurer can pay up to that amount — not necessarily the full amount. Less protective than agreed value. <br/><strong>Actual Cash Value:</strong> Market value minus depreciation. Terrible for classics. Never accept this for a collector car.</p>
</TipBox>

Coverage requires the vehicle to be garaged and not used as a daily driver.</p>

      <h2 id="grundy">Grundy — Best Value for Show Cars</h2>

      <div class="insurer-card">
        <div class="insurer-card-header">
          <div>
            <div class="insurer-name">Grundy</div>
            <div class="insurer-tagline">Since 1947 — the original specialty insurer</div>
          </div>
          <div class="insurer-badge">Best Value</div>
        </div>
        <div class="insurer-body">
          <p>Grundy has been insuring collector cars since 1947 — longer than any other specialty insurer. They pioneered the agreed value concept for classic cars and remain a top choice, especially for pure show cars with very limited use.</p>
          <p>Their key differentiator is pricing. Grundy tends to come in significantly lower than Hagerty on premium, particularly for cars driven fewer than 1,000 miles per year. They also offer <strong>no mileage restrictions</strong> on some policies — you get a policy limit instead of a mileage cap, which works well for cars that occasionally go to events.</p>
          <p>The tradeoff: their digital experience and online tools lag behind Hagerty. Getting a quote requires more manual steps, and their valuation process is less automated. For experienced collectors who know their car's value and just want solid, affordable coverage, Grundy is outstanding. For newer collectors who want the full ecosystem, Hagerty may serve better.</p>
        </div>
        <div class="insurer-pros-cons">

            <div class="pros-list-header">Strengths</div>

              <li>True agreed value since inception</li>
              <li>No mileage cap on some policies</li>
              <li>Excellent for low-use show cars</li>
              <li>Long track record, strong reputation</li>
            </ul>
          </div>
          <div class="cons-list">
            <div class="cons-list-header">Limitations</div>
            <ul>
              <li>Dated digital experience</li>
              <li>Less flexible for higher-mileage use</li>
              <li>Valuation process less automated</li>

      <h2 id="key-questions">Questions to Ask Any Insurer</h2>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>Is this agreed value or stated value?</strong> — Only accept agreed value for a classic</li>
        <li><strong>What are the mileage restrictions?</strong> — Know the exact limit before you sign</li>
        <li><strong>Is garaging required?</strong> — And what qualifies as "garaging"?</li>
        <li><strong>How are modifications handled?</strong> — Restomods, engine swaps, and upgrades need to be disclosed and covered</li>
        <li><strong>What's the claims process?</strong> — Ask specifically about total loss process and how value disputes are resolved</li>
        <li><strong>Can I use my regular mechanic?</strong> — Some classic insurers require shop approval on claims</li>
      </ul>
