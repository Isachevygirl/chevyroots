## Bed Accessories

### 🛞 BAKFlip MX4 Hard Folding Tonneau Cover — **10/10, buy again**

The single best accessory I've bought. I was skeptical of hard folding covers (soft roll-ups are cheaper and simpler) but the BAKFlip won me over the first time I got caught in a storm with the tailgate down and the load dry. The matte finish matches the ZR2's aesthetic, the seals are tight, and the three folding positions give you exactly the bed access you need without removing the cover.

### 🧼 Husky Liners WeatherBeater Bed Mat — **9/10**

Drop-in rubber bed mat that protects the factory spray-in liner from big sharp items (I'd scuffed my previous truck's liner with a tool box after six months). The WeatherBeater is thick, doesn't slide, and is cheap insurance for the spray-in.

### 🚚 Tie-Down Cleats (Factory Option) — **10/10, factory upgrade**

I paid the $150 factory upgrade for the extra tie-down cleats. Best $150 I've spent on a truck option. The stock cleats are fine, the extras turn the bed into an actual system.

## Interior

Already paid for itself. Keeps the factory carpet looking new through muddy boots, beach days, dog shuttle service, and one unfortunate coffee spill. If you have a truck, you should have WeatherTechs in it. That's my rule.

### 📦 Salex Console Organizer — **9/10**

The ZR2 has a massive center console that Chevy designed to be spacious and flexible. In practice, "flexible" means "everything slides around and you can never find anything." The Salex drop-in tray turns the console into a proper organizer with dedicated phone, wallet, fob, and snack compartments.

### 🎧 PAC AudioControl Bass Knob — **8/10**

Factory Bose system sounds good but lacks a convenient bass adjustment. A PAC bass knob mounted under the dash gives you on-the-fly subwoofer control. Small thing, daily quality-of-life improvement.

### 🔌 Dash Cam: BlackVue DR770X-2CH — **10/10, should be standard**

I'll put a dash cam in every vehicle I own going forward. The BlackVue records on ignition, parks itself in standby when you're away, and captures everything that matters. I've had three incidents where the dash cam footage was useful, including one where it settled a parking lot bump in 10 seconds.

<TipBox label="Dash Cam Pro Tip">
Hardwire it to the OBDII port with a voltage monitor, not to the cigarette lighter. You'll get parking mode without draining the truck battery, and you won't have a dangling cable in the footwell. Takes 30 minutes and a $20 kit.
</TipBox>

## Exterior Protection

### 🛡️ Paint Protection Film (Rocker Panels) — **10/10**

Did this at the same shop that did the rest of the PPF work. The rocker panels on a ZR2 take the worst of the rock chipping from the 33-inch tires. PPF'd rockers stay factory-new; unprotected rockers look thrashed in six months of off-road use. For under $400 at a good installer, this is the single best "protect your investment" move.

### 🎨 PPF on Hood and Front Fenders — **9/10**

Pricier than the rockers (around $800) but catches every rock strike on the highway. I do a lot of highway miles and the front of the truck stays showroom.

### 🚗 Mud Flaps (Front and Rear) — **8/10**

Stock is surprisingly good on the ZR2. I still added aftermarket rubber flaps because 33s throw mud and rocks hard enough to damage paint behind the wheels. Took 20 minutes to install.

## Lighting

### 💡 Rigid SR-Q Ditch Lights (Pair) — **9/10**

Small, flood-pattern ditch lights mounted to the A-pillars with a no-drill bracket. Actually useful for trail driving — they light up the edges of the trail where your headlights don't reach. Worth the money.

### 💡 Rigid 50" Light Bar — **3/10, returned**

Looked cool in the photos. Mounted it. Never turned it on. Took it off. The ZR2 factory headlights are so good that I couldn't find a real-world scenario where a 50" bar added anything meaningful. It was truck jewelry I didn't need. Don't make my mistake.

<WarningBox label="Light Bar Reality">
Light bars on modern trucks are almost always decoration. Factory LED headlights on a ZR2 are rated around 1,800 lumens per side. A 50" light bar adds raw lumens, but the distribution is flat and the beam pattern is designed for controlled environments (race stages), not real trails. Ditch lights for side coverage are usually the better investment.
</WarningBox>

### 💡 Amber DRL Conversion (KC HiLiTES) — **7/10**

Tints the daytime running lights amber. Looks great. No functional benefit. Pure aesthetic. If you like the look (I do), it's a $120 mod. If you don't care, skip it.

## Wheels & Tires

### 🛞 BFGoodrich KO3 All-Terrains (Replacement) — **Not yet, but planned**

The factory Wrangler Territory MTs are good mud-terrain-adjacent tires but loud on the highway. I'm planning to swap to KO3 all-terrains at the first tire change — they're quieter, last longer, and for my 80% on-road / 20% off-road split, they're the right tire. Will update this review when I pull the trigger.

### 🛞 Spare Tire Lock (Bolt-On) — **9/10**

Factory spare is exposed underneath the truck. A $40 lock is cheap insurance against spare tire theft, which is surprisingly common.

## Performance (Or Lack Thereof)

### ❌ K&N Cold Air Intake — **2/10, returned**

Lost torque feel. Got 3 horsepower and a louder induction sound. The sound gets old fast. Took it off after three weeks. Don't do it.

### ❌ Bigger Exhaust — **Didn't install, wasn't going to**

Considered a cat-back exhaust. Read enough "my truck sounds like a Harley I don't own" reviews to walk away. The factory ZR2 exhaust is well-tuned and has the right note. Aftermarket exhausts on modern emissions-tuned V8s rarely improve the sound beyond the first week.

### ✅ Range AFM/DFM Disabler — **Evaluating**

The 6.2 has Active Fuel Management that switches between 8 and 4 cylinders. The Range device disables AFM, which many owners believe prevents the lifter failure issue. I haven't installed it yet because I'm still inside the factory powertrain warranty and I don't want to void anything. Planning to add it when I'm out of coverage.

## Towing & Hitches

### 🪝 Curt Class IV Hitch Reinforcement Plate — **9/10**

I tow my dirt bike trailer regularly. Factory hitch is rated fine for what I'm doing, but the reinforcement plate adds piece of mind for consistent use. Cheap insurance.

### 🔌 Weight Distribution Hitch (for heavier loads) — **10/10**

The only way to safely tow at the ZR2's rated max is with a weight distribution hitch. I use a Curt TruTrack. If you're towing anything above 6,000 pounds, this is mandatory, not optional.

## Things I'm Planning But Haven't Bought Yet

- **Factory Chevrolet floor mats in the all-weather rubber** (replacing the WeatherTechs long-term — factory fitment is slightly better)
- **Bed-mounted cargo management system** for the dirt bike
- **A proper ARB compressor setup** for airing tires on the trail
- **PPF on the rear wheel arches** (the PPF shop I used only did fronts the first time)

## The Total I've Spent on Accessories

Adding it all up: about **$3,100** in accessories in the first six months. That's on top of the truck purchase price. Worth every dollar on the tonneau, WeatherTechs, PPF, dash cam, and ditch lights. Wasted money on the light bar and intake.

<CrystalTake>
The rule of thumb I've learned: **the best accessory money is protective** (tonneau, PPF, floor liners, dash cam, bed mat). The second best is **practical** (tie-downs, console organizer, WD hitch). The worst is **aesthetic** (light bar, intake, sound mods) unless you love the look and you've factored in that it's pure aesthetic.

The ZR2 is already a great truck from the factory. The best mods make it easier to live with, not louder or flashier.
</CrystalTake>
