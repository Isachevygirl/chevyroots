      <h2 id="what-you-get">What the GM Military Discount Actually Gets You</h2>

      <p>The GM Military Appreciation Program provides <strong>below-invoice pricing</strong> on most eligible GM vehicles. Specifically:</p>

      <ul>
        <li>You pay a fixed price that is typically <strong>1–5% below dealer invoice</strong>, depending on the model and trim</li>
        <li>The discount is structured as a set price formula — it's not dealer-negotiated, it comes directly from GM</li>
        <li>On a $45,000 Silverado, this translates to roughly <strong>$1,500–$3,000 off</strong> versus what most buyers pay</li>
        <li>On high-trim Tahoes or Suburbans, the savings can be <strong>$3,000–$5,000+</strong></li>
      </ul>

      <p>This is not a coupon. It's a structured pricing arrangement between GM and the dealer. The dealer is required to honor it. If a dealer tells you "we can't do that," find a different dealer.</p>

      <h2 id="stacking">The Real Move: How to Stack Discounts</h2>

      <p>The military discount is a great starting point. But buyers who know the system don't stop there. The GM Military Appreciation discount can be <strong>combined with</strong>:</p>

      <ul>
        <li><strong>Current GM rebates and cash back offers</strong> — check Chevrolet.com for active offers in your zip code. These are in addition to the military price.</li>
        <li><strong>GM loyalty bonus</strong> — if you currently own a GM vehicle, you may qualify for an additional $500–$1,500 loyalty incentive on top of both the military price and any rebates.</li>
        <li><strong>Dealer discount</strong> — even with military pricing, dealers can still discount from their side. Military pricing doesn't lock the floor — it's a starting point below invoice. You can still negotiate dealer participation further.</li>
        <li><strong>Financing incentives</strong> — GM Financial occasionally offers military-specific APR deals. Ask specifically about GM Financial military financing rates.</li>
      </ul>

<TipBox label="Stack Example">
<p>Military price ($2,500 off invoice) + $2,000 GM cash rebate + $750 loyalty bonus + dealer discount = $5,000–$6,000+ off MSRP on a Silverado. This is a real-world achievable stack for a prepared buyer.</p>
</TipBox>

      <h2 id="verify">How to Verify: ID.me and the Process</h2>

      <p>GM uses <strong>ID.me</strong> to verify military eligibility. The process is straightforward and takes about 10 minutes:</p>

      <ol class="step-list">
        <li><strong>Go to the GM Military Appreciation Program page</strong> at gmfleet.com/incentives/military-appreciation.html or through Chevrolet.com. Look for the "Verify Eligibility" or "Get Discount" button.</li>
        <li><strong>Create or log into your ID.me account.</strong> ID.me is a third-party identity verification service used by government agencies and major employers. If you've verified through VA.gov or other federal services, you may already have an account.</li>
        <li><strong>Verify your military status.</strong> You'll need a military ID, DD-214 (for veterans), or other documentation depending on your status. The verification is digital and usually instant if your records are in the Defense Manpower Data Center.</li>
        <li><strong>Receive your Authorization Number.</strong> Once verified, GM issues a unique authorization number tied to your identity and vehicle purchase. This number is valid for a specific window (usually 30 days).</li>
        <li><strong>Present the authorization number at the dealer.</strong> Give it to the sales manager before any negotiation begins. They look it up in GM's system and the pricing adjusts automatically.</li>
      </ol>

      <h2 id="at-the-dealer">Using the Discount at the Dealership: Step-by-Step</h2>

      <ol class="step-list">
        <li><strong>Call ahead and ask if the dealer participates in the GM Military Appreciation Program.</strong> All GM dealers should, but confirming saves you a trip.</li>
        <li><strong>Present your authorization number before any price discussion.</strong> Don't let the dealer start a negotiation and then introduce the military discount later — some try to absorb it rather than add it on top of what they were already offering.</li>
        <li><strong>Request a full breakdown of all incentives separately.</strong> Ask to see the military price, any active rebates, and the dealer's separate discount listed as individual line items. Bundling hides money.</li>
        <li><strong>Get the out-the-door price.</strong> Negotiate from the military-adjusted price, not MSRP. Then confirm the final total including tax, title, registration, and any dealer fees.</li>
        <li><strong>Don't let them roll the trade into the deal until the price is set.</strong> Handle trade-in value separately, after you've locked the vehicle price.</li>
      </ol>

      <h2 id="mistakes">Common Mistakes to Avoid</h2>

      <ul>
        <li><strong>Not stacking the discount with active rebates.</strong> The military price doesn't replace cash back — it stacks with it. Many buyers don't ask and leave $2,000+ on the table.</li>
        <li><strong>Accepting the first offer.</strong> Military pricing sets the floor — dealers can still improve from there. Treat it as your starting point, not the final word.</li>
        <li><strong>Not getting competing quotes.</strong> Contact at least two or three dealers. Military pricing is uniform from GM, but dealer fees, add-ons, and trade-in valuations vary enormously.</li>
        <li><strong>Waiting until the authorization expires.</strong> Your authorization number has a time limit. Don't generate it until you're ready to buy within the valid window.</li>
        <li><strong>Forgetting household member eligibility.</strong> A spouse buying a family vehicle qualifies under the eligible service member's status. Don't leave this program on the table because you assumed only the service member could use it.</li>
      </ul>

<WarningBox label="Don't Do This">
<p>Do not let a dealer bundle your military discount into a global "deal" they present as their best offer. Insist on seeing the military price adjustment as a separate, identified line item so you can confirm it's actually been applied and nothing else has been secretly inflated to compensate.</p>
</WarningBox>
