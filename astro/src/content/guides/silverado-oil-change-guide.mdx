<h2 id="intro">Why Doing It Yourself Matters</h2>
      <p>The Chevy Silverado is America's best-selling truck for a reason — it's built to work hard. But no matter which engine you're running, nothing protects that investment like clean oil on schedule. Dealer oil changes cost $80–$140 depending on engine. Doing it yourself takes about 30 minutes and costs half that. More importantly, <strong>you know it was done right.</strong></p>
      <p>This guide covers every current Silverado engine: the turbocharged 2.7L four-cylinder, the 5.3L V8 EcoTec3, the 6.2L V8 EcoTec3, and the 3.0L Duramax inline-six diesel. Each has different oil specs, capacities, and filter part numbers. Use the spec table to find your engine, then follow the steps.</p>

<TipBox label="Quick Reference">
<p>All current Silverado gasoline engines require <strong>Dexos1 Gen 3</strong> certified oil. The Duramax diesel takes <strong>Dexos D</strong>. Using the wrong spec oil can void your GM warranty.</p>
</TipBox>

      <h2 id="steps">Step-by-Step Oil Change</h2>
      <p>These steps apply to all Silverado engines. The only things that change are the oil quantity, filter part number, and drain plug torque spec (see tables above).</p>

      <ol class="step-list">
        <li><strong>Warm up the engine 2–3 minutes</strong> — warm oil drains faster and carries more contaminants out with it. Don't run it hot or you'll burn yourself.</li>
        <li><strong>Raise and support the truck safely</strong> — drive up on ramps, or jack it up and place it on jack stands. Never work under a truck supported only by a floor jack.</li>
        <li><strong>Locate and loosen the drain plug</strong> — it's on the bottom of the oil pan. Position your drain pan under it before you pull the plug. The oil will shoot sideways initially.</li>
        <li><strong>Pull the plug and let it drain completely</strong> — 5 minutes minimum. Inspect the plug and gasket for damage while you wait.</li>
        <li><strong>Remove the old filter</strong> — use an oil filter wrench for extra grip. Have rags ready; some oil will spill when it comes loose. On V8s the filter is on the passenger side near the front of the block.</li>
        <li><strong>Install the new filter</strong> — lightly oil the new filter's gasket with fresh oil. Thread it on by hand until the gasket seats, then an additional 3/4 turn. Do not use a wrench to tighten — hand tight is correct.</li>
        <li><strong>Reinstall the drain plug</strong> — thread it in by hand first to avoid cross-threading, then torque to spec (see table above).</li>
        <li><strong>Add fresh oil through the top</strong> — use the capacity listed in the spec table. Start with 1 quart less, then check the dipstick and top up.</li>
        <li><strong>Run the engine 30 seconds, shut off, wait 5 minutes</strong> — check for leaks at the drain plug and filter, then re-check the dipstick. Top up if needed to the "Full" line.</li>
        <li><strong>Reset the oil life monitor</strong> — see section below.</li>
      </ol>

<CrystalTake>
<p>The V8 Silverados hold 8 quarts — that's two full 5-qt jugs minus 2 quarts. Buy two jugs, pour all of the first one in, then add from the second while checking the dipstick. You'll have leftover oil for next time. <strong>Don't pour all 8 quarts in blindly</strong> — overfilling damages seals and can hydro-lock a cylinder on startup.</p>
</CrystalTake>

      <h2 id="reset">Resetting the Oil Life Monitor</h2>
      <p>The Silverado's DIC (Driver Information Center) tracks oil life based on engine conditions, not just mileage. It needs to be manually reset after every oil change. The method varies slightly by generation but follows the same logic.</p>

      <h3>2019–2026 Silverado (T1 Platform)</h3>
      <ol class="step-list">
        <li><strong>Press the Home button</strong> on the steering wheel to bring up the main DIC menu.</li>
        <li><strong>Navigate to "Vehicle Information"</strong> using the up/down arrows on the steering wheel.</li>
        <li><strong>Find "Oil Life"</strong> — it will show the current remaining percentage.</li>
        <li><strong>Press and hold the checkmark/OK button</strong> for 3–5 seconds until you see "Oil Life Reset Confirmed" or the percentage resets to 100%.</li>
      </ol>

<TipBox label="Alternative Method">
<p>Turn ignition to ON (engine off). Press the accelerator pedal fully to the floor and release it <strong>three times</strong> within five seconds. The oil life indicator will flash and then reset. This works on most 2014–2026 Silverados if you can't navigate the DIC menus.</p>
</TipBox>

      <h2 id="interval">How Often Should You Change Your Oil?</h2>
      <p>GM's official guidance for the Silverado is "follow the oil life monitor." In practice, that typically works out to:</p>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>Normal driving:</strong> 7,500–10,000 miles with full synthetic Dexos1 Gen 3</li>
        <li><strong>Severe service (towing, short trips, dusty conditions):</strong> 5,000–7,500 miles</li>
        <li><strong>3.0L Duramax diesel:</strong> Up to 7,500 miles with Dexos D — the diesel is actually very tolerant of extended intervals with the right oil</li>
      </ul>
      <p>The old "every 3,000 miles" rule is a myth for modern full-synthetic oils. You're wasting money and oil if you're still doing that with a 2019+ Silverado.</p>
