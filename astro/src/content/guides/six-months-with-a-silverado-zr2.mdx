Six months with the ZR2. Almost 9,000 miles. A handful of off-road days. One long highway trip. A winter cold spell. A few dealer visits that turned out to be nothing. And a growing list of "huh, I didn't expect that" notes that I've been keeping in a note on my phone the whole time.

This is the honest review. The stuff I'd actually tell a friend if they were calling me asking whether they should buy one.

## The Short Version

I'd buy this truck again. The DSSV dampers are everything I hoped they'd be. The 6.2 has grown on me from "impressive" to "this is my favorite engine I've ever owned." The off-road capability is real, not a marketing bullet point. The interior is better than it gets credit for.

But there are things I'd do differently if I were ordering again — and some things I wish I'd known before I walked in the dealership the first time. That's what the rest of this article is about.

## What's Exceeded My Expectations

### The Multimatic DSSV Dampers

I knew these would be good. I didn't know they'd be *this* good.

The way the truck settles after a highway lane change, the way it doesn't pitch under braking, the way you can push it into a corner entry faster than you should be able to push a 5,500-pound truck — it makes driving this thing on-road genuinely entertaining, which is the last thing I expected from an off-road-focused trim. I've driven plenty of lifted trucks. Most of them feel like lifted trucks: vague steering, nose-dive on braking, a slightly seasick wallow over highway expansion joints. The ZR2 feels like a well-tuned SUV.

<Pullquote>The DSSV dampers alone are worth the price difference from the TrailBoss.</Pullquote>

### The 6.2L V8 on the 10-Speed

The combination of the 6.2 and the 10-speed automatic is better than I gave it credit for. The 10-speed gets some grief — and I'll talk about the shudder issue below — but with the 6.2's torque curve, you basically never notice it. It's in the right gear at the right time, it downshifts intelligently, and the throttle mapping in Sport mode is sharp enough to make the truck feel quick at legal-for-the-highway speeds.

### The Interior, Especially on Long Drives

I did an 11-hour drive two months in. I'd psychologically prepared myself for it to be uncomfortable — trucks usually are. It wasn't. The seats hold you in position without squeezing, the center console armrest is at the right height, the climate control keeps the cabin sane, and the stereo is well above average for factory. Six months in and I've driven this truck for hours at a stretch multiple times with no fatigue issues.

### Off-Road Capability I'll Never Fully Use

I live in a place with rocky trails, sand washes, and the occasional decent mud situation. The ZR2 shrugs at all of it. The front locker combined with the Multimatic dampers soaking up big hits means I've been in situations where I was waiting for the truck to complain and it just... didn't. I'm not saying it can't be stuck. I'm saying it's going to take more than what I'm currently comfortable attempting to stop it.

## What's Surprised Me (In Good and Bad Ways)

### The Fuel Economy Is Worse Than Advertised

I'm averaging 15.8 mpg combined. EPA says 15/17/16. I'm in the ballpark, but the highway number is more like 17–18 in real life, not the 19 I hoped for. If you're coming from a 1500 with the 5.3 or the 3.0 Duramax, prepare to adjust your mental model about how often you're going to be at the pump.

<TipBox label="Fuel Cost Reality">
At an average of 15.8 mpg and ~12,000 miles a year, you're buying about 760 gallons annually. At $3.50 a gallon, that's ~$2,660 a year in fuel. The diesel TrailBoss at 22 real mpg would save me ~$800 a year. Over 5 years that's $4,000. Something to weigh against the ZR2's other advantages.
</TipBox>

### The 6.2 Wants Premium Fuel

The 6.2 calls for 91 octane minimum. I knew this going in but didn't fully process it until I started paying for it. Running 87 in the 6.2 technically works in the sense that the truck won't blow up, but it will pull timing, you'll lose power, and fuel economy will get worse — which is the opposite of savings. If you're shopping a ZR2 or any 6.2 Silverado, budget for premium fuel permanently.

### The 10-Speed Shudder Is Real (But Fixable)

Around 3,500 miles I started feeling a very faint shudder at light-throttle cruising between 40 and 55 mph. It's the known torque converter clutch slip issue GM's been addressing with updated Dexron HP fluid. A trip to the dealer, a fluid exchange, and the shudder was gone. Covered under the powertrain warranty. See our <a href="/guides/silverado-transmission-shudder-fix/">transmission shudder fix guide</a> for the full background.

<WarningBox label="Don't Ignore It">
If you feel the shudder on your truck, get it fixed under warranty. GM has TSBs addressing it and the dealer should flush and refill with the updated fluid at no cost while you're in the coverage period. Don't let it go — repeated slip will eventually damage the torque converter, and a torque converter replacement out of warranty runs $1,500–$3,500.
</WarningBox>

### The Ride Is Not Magic on Washboard

The DSSVs are phenomenal on pavement and on big off-road hits. But on washboard dirt roads — the high-frequency, low-amplitude corrugations you get on maintained but unpaved roads — they're just OK. Not better than a well-setup TrailBoss. A little trial and error with tire pressure helps. Airing down to 28 psi on the trail is a meaningful improvement over 35 psi road pressure.

### The Stock Tires Are Louder Than I Wanted

The 33-inch Goodyear Wrangler Territory MTs are great off-road and honestly decent on-road, but they're louder than the all-terrains on a TrailBoss would be. At highway speeds you notice it. It's not bad. It's just different from a 1500 running stock street tires.

## The Accessories I Regret

Two of them:

1. **A front light bar I mounted and then never turned on.** I thought I'd want it. I have almost zero real-world scenarios where the factory headlights plus a pair of fog lights don't cover what I need. $300 for a piece of truck jewelry that's getting removed.

2. **An aftermarket cold air intake.** I got excited, installed it, picked up maybe 3 horsepower, lost some low-end torque feel, and made the truck sound louder in a way that's fun for 30 minutes and annoying for 3 hours. The stock airbox on the 6.2 is well designed. Save your $400.

<CrystalTake>
The best money you'll spend on a ZR2 is the money you DON'T spend. The truck is good from the factory. The second best money is on protection — tonneau cover, floor liners, paint protection film on the rockers where the tires throw rocks. The worst money is on sound/induction/lightbar mods that feel exciting in the catalog and add exactly zero to how the truck drives or works for you.
</CrystalTake>

## What I'd Do Differently

**If I were ordering a ZR2 again today:**

1. **Add the 6.2 Performance package** (bigger brakes, different axle ratio) — I skipped it and I think about it every time I'm doing spirited driving
2. **Skip the sunroof** — I use it maybe twice a month and it eats a chunk of headroom
3. **Order in a color other than silver** — the silver hides the beautiful lines of this truck in low light. A darker color (Northsky Blue Metallic, or the stealth black) would have been a better choice aesthetically
4. **Get the bed liner from the factory, not aftermarket** — factory is bonded, aftermarket pops a seam over time. The factory upcharge is worth it.
5. **Negotiate harder on the extended warranty** — I paid too much in the finance office. See our <a href="/guides/chevy-extended-warranty/">extended warranty guide</a> for what I should have paid.

**What I'd tell a friend who's shopping one:**

- Drive a TrailBoss and a ZR2 back to back. On pavement, at normal speeds, on a route with some corners. If the ZR2 doesn't feel clearly better, buy the TrailBoss and save the money.
- Don't finance at the dealer's rate without shopping it first. My credit union beat the dealer's offer by 1.4 percentage points on the same loan term.
- Get the truck in your preferred color, not whatever's on the lot. Dealers will trade units.
- Read the window sticker line by line before signing. "Protection package" and "market adjustment" are the most common scams in the finance office.

## The Bottom Line

Six months in, the ZR2 is the best truck I've ever owned. It's also the most expensive truck I've ever owned, and I'm not going to pretend the payment isn't real. But I'd do it again, and I'd recommend the truck — with eyes open about fuel costs, premium gas requirements, and the fact that the off-road capability is wasted on 90% of owners.
