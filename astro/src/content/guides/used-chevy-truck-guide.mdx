      <h2 id="silverado-k2">2015–2018 Silverado 1500: Proven and Affordable</h2>

      <div class="year-card">
        <div class="year-range">2015–2018</div>
        <div class="year-model">Silverado 1500 (K2XX)</div>
        <div class="year-price">Market Range: &#126;$15,000–$24,000</div>
        <div class="year-body">
          <p>The K2XX generation Silverado has one of the best reliability reputations in the full-size truck segment. These are battle-tested, simple trucks. The 5.3L V8 paired with the 6-speed automatic is not exciting — but it works, and it works for a long time. Many fleet examples have 150,000+ miles and still run strong.</p>
          <p>The downside is that <strong>Active Fuel Management (AFM)</strong> — GM's cylinder deactivation system — was standard on the 5.3L V8 in these years and has a known track record of causing oil consumption issues and occasional lifter failures over time. This isn't universal, but it's worth a targeted pre-purchase inspection. If you find a K2XX Silverado with AFM deleted (a common mod), that's a green flag, not a red one.</p>
          <p>The 6.2L V8 option in this generation — found in LTZ and High Country trims — is exceptional. If you can find a 6.2 at this price point, it's one of the best truck engine deals anywhere in the used market.</p>

      <h2 id="inspect">What to Inspect on Any Used Chevy Truck</h2>

      <p>Before committing to any used truck, run these checks — ideally with a pre-purchase inspection from an independent mechanic, not the selling dealer.</p>

      <div class="inspect-grid">
        <div class="inspect-item">
          <div class="inspect-title">Frame &amp; Undercarriage</div>
          <div class="inspect-body">Look for rust perforation (not surface rust) on the frame rails, especially on salt-belt vehicles. Check the differential housing, driveshaft carrier bearing, and exhaust hangers for corrosion.</div>
        </div>
        <div class="inspect-item">
          <div class="inspect-title">Engine Oil</div>
          <div class="inspect-body">Pull the dipstick. Oil should be amber-brown, not black or milky. Milky oil means coolant intrusion — walk away. Check the oil cap underside for white residue.</div>
        </div>
        <div class="inspect-item">
          <div class="inspect-title">Transmission</div>
          <div class="inspect-body">During the test drive, feel for shudder at highway cruise (30–50 mph TCC lockup range). Any vibration there needs investigation before buying. Also check transmission fluid — should be pink/red, not dark brown.</div>
        </div>
        <div class="inspect-item">
          <div class="inspect-title">4WD System</div>
          <div class="inspect-body">Test all 4WD modes: 2H, 4H, 4L. Listen for grinding or clunking engagement. Test the transfer case for binding in 4WD on a tight turn in a parking lot.</div>
        </div>
        <div class="inspect-item">
          <div class="inspect-title">Bed &amp; Cab</div>
          <div class="inspect-body">Check for evidence of heavy hauling (scratched bed floor, bent tie-down hooks), trailer brake controller wiring, and frame hitch damage. These indicate how the truck was actually used.</div>
        </div>
        <div class="inspect-item">
          <div class="inspect-title">Service History</div>
          <div class="inspect-body">Carfax and AutoCheck show title history but not maintenance. Ask specifically for oil change records. A truck with zero service documentation is a risk regardless of how it looks or drives.</div>
        </div>
      </div>

      <h2 id="mileage">Mileage Sweet Spot: 40K–80K Miles</h2>

      <p>The best bang for your dollar on a used Chevy truck lives in the <strong>40,000–80,000 mile range</strong>. Here's why:</p>

      <ul>
        <li><strong>Under 40K:</strong> The steepest depreciation has already occurred, but prices are still high. You're paying a premium for low miles that usually don't matter much on a truck this reliable.</li>
        <li><strong>40K–80K:</strong> Substantial depreciation has happened. The truck has been broken in. Any early manufacturing defects have surfaced and been dealt with. You're getting the most value per dollar.</li>
        <li><strong>Over 100K:</strong> Prices drop significantly but you're closer to major maintenance items: water pump, timing chain service, transmission fluid service. Budget for these proactively — they're not failures, they're maintenance.</li>
      </ul>

      <h2 id="cpo">CPO vs Private Sale</h2>

      <p><strong>Certified Pre-Owned (CPO)</strong> from a Chevy dealer means the truck has passed a multi-point inspection and comes with an extended factory warranty (typically extending to 5 years/100,000 miles from original sale). You're paying a premium — typically $1,500–$3,000 over comparable private-party price — but you get warranty coverage and peace of mind.</p>

      <p><strong>Private party</strong> purchases are usually 10–15% cheaper than dealer prices, but you assume all the risk. This is where a pre-purchase inspection is non-negotiable. Spend $150–$200 on an independent PPI and treat it as insurance. It's the best $150 you'll spend in the transaction.</p>

<TipBox label="CPO Tip">
<p>GM's CPO warranty is transferable if you buy from a dealer. But confirm whether remaining factory warranty transferred already — some CPO vehicles are close to the mileage cutoff by the time they reach you.</p>
</TipBox>

      <h2 id="financing">Financing Tips for Used Trucks</h2>

      <ul>
        <li><strong>Get pre-approved before you shop.</strong> Your bank or credit union can pre-approve you in hours. Walk in knowing your rate and max monthly payment — don't let a dealer be your only financing option.</li>
        <li><strong>Credit unions typically beat banks</strong> on used auto loan rates. If you're not a member of one, many allow you to join for $5–$25. The rate savings over a 60-month loan can be $1,000–$2,000.</li>
        <li><strong>Avoid 72–84 month terms on used vehicles.</strong> The lower payment feels good until the truck needs a $3,000 repair at month 50 and you still owe $18,000 on a vehicle worth $12,000. Keep it to 48–60 months maximum on a used truck.</li>
        <li><strong>Don't skip GAP coverage if you finance high.</strong> If you're putting less than 20% down, GAP insurance protects you if the truck is totaled. Buy it through your own insurer, not the dealer's F&I office.</li>
        <li><strong>Factor in total cost of ownership:</strong> insurance rates (trucks run higher than cars in most states), fuel, and anticipated maintenance when calculating what you can actually afford.</li>
      </ul>
