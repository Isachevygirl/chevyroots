Chevy at the Ballpark:
Official Vehicle of Major League Baseball

Twenty years. Thirty stadiums. One Silverado handed to the World Series MVP every October. Here's the full story of Chevrolet's partnership with MLB — and why it's one of the greatest sports sponsorships in automotive history.

By Crystal @ ChevyRoots
April 2026

The Partnership

Chevrolet became the Official Vehicle of Major League Baseball in 2005 — and twenty-plus years later, it's still one of the most visible and enduring brand relationships in American sports. Think about what has changed in that time: stadiums have come and gone, dynasties have been built and broken, streaming has completely disrupted how we watch the game. But every fall, when the confetti flies and the MVP walks to the podium, there's a Chevy on the field.

Chevy Branding Visible

The partnership operates at every level of the sport. Chevy branding appears on broadcast graphics — those lower-third overlays during national TV broadcasts — as well as in-stadium signage at all 30 ballparks, across MLB's digital platforms, and on official league merchandise and communications. When you're watching a game on Fox or ESPN and you see a branded segment or a sponsored replay, there's a real chance the Chevy bowtie is in that frame.

But the crown jewel of the whole deal — the thing that makes it iconic — is the World Series MVP Award. More on that in a minute.

Why This Partnership Works

Baseball and Chevrolet share the same DNA: quintessentially American, deeply regional, built on loyalty that passes down through generations. A kid who grows up going to Astros games with their dad doesn't just become an Astros fan — they pick up all of it. The food, the rituals, the truck in the parking lot. Chevy understood this alignment long before most brands figured out sports sponsorship.

This is widely regarded as one of the largest sports sponsorships in the automotive industry. MLB's reach — over 70 million attendees per season pre-pandemic, plus hundreds of millions of broadcast viewers — gives Chevy consistent, long-form exposure across a demographic that actually buys trucks and SUVs. It's not just logo placement. It's cultural integration.

The World Series MVP Award

Every year, after the final out of the World Series, one player is named MVP of the Fall Classic. And every year, on the field in the aftermath of a championship, Chevrolet hands that player the keys to a brand new vehicle. Usually a Silverado. Sometimes something even more special.

PHOTO — Jeremy Peña or Corey Seager on the field
MVP trophy in one hand, Silverado keys in the other
Camera flashes, crowd in background — the big moment

The presentation happens right there in the stadium, with the championship crowd still going, confetti still in the air. It's one of those moments that has become part of the World Series ritual — you watch for the trophy, the champagne, and the Chevy. In order.

Stephen Strasburg in 2019 didn't just get a car — he got the first Corvette C8 ever handed to a civilian. That was the public debut of an icon. Chevy used the World Series stage to unveil a generation.

— ChevyRoots Editorial

The 2019 moment deserves its own paragraph. Strasburg's Nationals had just beaten the Astros in a wild Game 7, and when Chevy rolled out the Corvette C8 — the mid-engine redesign that enthusiasts had been waiting decades for — the whole world was watching. That's not just sponsorship. That's a product launch on the biggest stage in baseball. Bold move. Perfect execution.

Corey Seager is now a two-time recipient, winning in 2020 with the Dodgers and again in 2023 with the Rangers. Two Silverados, two championships, two different franchises. If you're keeping score, he's winning at life.

A Note on the 2022 Astros

Jeremy Peña won the World Series MVP in 2022 — the rookie shortstop who replaced a Hall of Famer and then went out and outplayed the entire postseason. He got a Silverado on the field at Minute Maid Park in Houston. More on what that means to this particular ChevyRoots writer below.

Chevy at Every Stadium

One of the ongoing projects at ChevyRoots is documenting the Chevy presence at all 30 MLB ballparks. Every stadium in the league carries Chevy branding in some form — signage, digital boards, sponsored sections, concourse installations, local dealer partnerships. The goal is to build a field guide: what to look for, where to find it, and what the Chevy experience looks like market by market.

What to Look For

  • Chevy signage locations — outfield walls, foul poles, video board lower thirds during play
  • Chevy-sponsored sections — some parks have branded home run areas, bleacher sections, or club spaces
  • Local dealer tie-ins — regional dealers often run activations, giveaways, and tailgate partnerships
  • Broadcast overlays — when the camera cuts to a sponsored replay or stat, check the logo
  • Parking lot activations — pregame events, test drive opportunities, tailgate setups near main gates

We're visiting stadiums and documenting the Chevy presence. Follow along as this guide fills out throughout the 2026 season — every visit gets logged with photos, notes on placement, and the full tailgate situation.

2026: MLB + Chevy + America 250

Here's something that doesn't happen very often: a perfect convergence of a brand campaign, a national milestone, and a 162-game season that touches every corner of the country.

2026 is America's 250th birthday. Chevy's "See the USA" campaign — a direct callback to the Dinah Shore jingle that became one of the most recognizable advertising lines in automotive history — is running full force. And MLB's season runs April through October, hitting cities from Miami to Seattle and everywhere in between.

The ultimate 2026 road trip: See the USA, watch baseball, drive a Chevy. You could start in March training camps and finish at the World Series in October. That's an American year.

— Crystal, ChevyRoots

The logistics write themselves. MLB's schedule crosses nearly every campaign destination on the America 250 map. You can plan a summer road trip that hits national monuments, historic sites, small towns, and ballparks — all from behind the wheel of a Silverado or Tahoe. It's exactly the kind of trip Chevy was built for, in a year that was made for it.

ChevyRoots is actively building out the America 250 Road Trip Guide alongside this MLB content. The overlap is intentional — we see this as the same story told from two different angles. Baseball is America. Chevy is America. 2026 is the year you put it all together.

Crystal's MLB Connection

C
Crystal — ChevyRoots Founder
Houston Native  ·  Lifelong Astros Fan  ·  Chevy Driver

I grew up in Houston. The Astros aren't a team I picked — they're a team I was born into. Minute Maid Park, the Orbit mascot, the orange and navy that's been in my house since before I can remember. Baseball and Houston are the same thing to me.

In 2022, the Astros won the World Series. And Jeremy Peña — the rookie who wasn't supposed to be there, who replaced a legend and then outperformed everyone in October — won MVP. They handed him a Silverado on the field. In Houston.

I own a Chevy. I'm from Houston. The truck and the trophy ended up in the same place at the same time. That's not just a sponsorship moment. That's the brand I've been building this site around, crystallized in one image on the biggest stage in baseball.

"Watching a Houston boy get handed the keys to a Chevy on the biggest stage in baseball? That's my whole brand in one moment."

The 2026 content plan on this front is straightforward: Orioles games at Camden Yards (close to home, great stadium, strong Chevy presence), Nationals games in DC (easy daytime trips, good for game day content), and ideally one or two Astros road trips when Houston comes to the mid-Atlantic region.

Every visit gets documented — parking situation, tailgate setup, Chevy signage spotted, game day energy. This is field research as much as it is fandom. The goal is a real, lived-in guide to what the Chevy x MLB experience looks like from a fan's perspective, not just a sponsorship press release.
