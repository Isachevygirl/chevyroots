Section 179 Tax Deduction: Which Chevys Qualify and How Much You Can Write Off

By Crystal & ChevyRoots Staff
Updated April 2026
7 min read
~1,000 words

What Is Section 179?

Section 179 of the U.S. tax code allows businesses to deduct the full purchase price of qualifying equipment and vehicles in the year they're placed in service — instead of depreciating them over several years. For business owners who need a truck or large SUV, it's one of the most powerful tax tools available.

The key phrase is "placed in service." The vehicle must be purchased and actually used for business before December 31 of the tax year you're claiming it. You can't buy it in December and leave it in the driveway — it has to be put to work.

Important Disclaimer

This guide is for informational purposes only. Tax laws change, and your specific situation involves factors we can't assess here. Always consult a licensed CPA or tax professional before making vehicle purchase decisions based on Section 179. This is not tax advice.

The SUV Cap

Many Chevy SUVs fall under the $30,500 SUV deduction cap — not the full $1.22M limit. This cap applies to "luxury SUVs" (vehicles over 6,000 lbs GVWR that aren't built on a dedicated truck chassis). True trucks (Silverado, Colorado) may qualify for the full deduction. The specific IRS rules depend on body type, not just weight — confirm with your CPA.

Which Chevys Qualify?

The IRS rule is straightforward: the vehicle must have a Gross Vehicle Weight Rating (GVWR) over 6,000 lbs to qualify. GVWR is the maximum loaded weight the vehicle is rated for — not curb weight. You can find it on the driver-side door jamb sticker.

Qualify (GVWR Over 6,000 lbs)
  • Silverado 1500 (all engines, all configs)
  • Silverado 2500HD / 3500HD
  • Tahoe (all trims)
  • Suburban (all trims)
  • Colorado (most configs — verify GVWR)
  • Express 1500 / 2500 Van
  • Blazer EV (verify GVWR — borderline)
Do NOT Qualify (GVWR Under 6,000 lbs)
  • Equinox (GVWR ~4,400–4,700 lbs)
  • Trax (GVWR ~3,600 lbs)
  • Malibu (discontinued — GVWR ~3,600 lbs)
  • Blazer (gas/non-EV — verify, varies by trim)
  • Trailblazer (GVWR ~3,900 lbs)
  • Bolt EV / EUV (GVWR ~3,900 lbs)

The Business Use Requirement

This is where people get into trouble. Section 179 requires the vehicle to be used more than 50% for business purposes in the year it's placed in service. If business use drops below 50% in a subsequent year, you may have to recapture part of the deduction as income.

The IRS doesn't just take your word for it. Here's what you need to document:

  • Mileage log: Record date, destination, business purpose, and odometer reading for every business trip
  • Business purpose: "Met with client" or "delivered supplies to job site" — vague entries won't hold up in an audit
  • Personal vs. business split: Commuting to a fixed workplace does NOT count as business use
  • Adequate records: The IRS requires contemporaneous records — logging miles retroactively from memory is not sufficient
The Commuting Trap

Driving from home to your regular place of business is considered personal commuting — not business use — even if you use the truck to haul equipment. Only miles driven for business purposes after you arrive at your regular workplace (or from home if you have no regular workplace) count. Many business owners overestimate their business use percentage because they count commuting miles.

How to Claim the Deduction

  1. Purchase and place the vehicle in service before December 31 of the tax year. It must be used for business at least once before year-end.
  2. Determine your GVWR from the driver-side door jamb sticker. Confirm it exceeds 6,000 lbs.
  3. Calculate your business use percentage from your mileage log. Must exceed 50%.
  4. Complete IRS Form 4562 (Depreciation and Amortization). Your CPA will handle this, but it's the form where Section 179 elections are made.
  5. Apply the deduction to your business income. Section 179 cannot create a net operating loss — if the deduction exceeds your business income, the remainder carries forward to future years.
  6. Maintain records for at least 3–5 years after filing. The IRS can audit back three years under normal circumstances.

What Does This Actually Save?

Assuming a 30% effective tax rate (federal + state), here's a rough look at potential savings on a qualifying vehicle purchase:

Vehicle Purchase Price Max 179 Deduction Tax Savings (30% rate)
Silverado 1500 LTZ (100% business) $58,000 $58,000 (full) ~$17,400
Tahoe Premier (100% business) $67,000 $30,500 (SUV cap) ~$9,150
Silverado 2500HD (100% business) $72,000 $72,000 (full) ~$21,600
Silverado 1500 (60% business use) $58,000 $34,800 (60% of price) ~$10,440
Silverado vs. Tahoe — The Tax Difference

The Silverado 1500 is classified as a "light truck" — not an SUV — so it can potentially qualify for the full Section 179 deduction on 100% business use, not just the $30,500 SUV cap. The Tahoe is classified as a large SUV and is capped. If you're on the fence between a truck and a large SUV for your business, this is a real financial consideration worth running by your CPA.
