How to Change the Oil on Your Chevy Silverado (Every Engine)

By Crystal & ChevyRoots Staff
Updated April 2026
8 min read
~1,200 words

Why Doing It Yourself Matters

The Chevy Silverado is America's best-selling truck for a reason — it's built to work hard. But no matter which engine you're running, nothing protects that investment like clean oil on schedule. Dealer oil changes cost $80–$140 depending on engine. Doing it yourself takes about 30 minutes and costs half that. More importantly, you know it was done right.

This guide covers every current Silverado engine: the turbocharged 2.7L four-cylinder, the 5.3L V8 EcoTec3, the 6.2L V8 EcoTec3, and the 3.0L Duramax inline-six diesel. Each has different oil specs, capacities, and filter part numbers. Use the spec table to find your engine, then follow the steps.

Quick Reference

All current Silverado gasoline engines require Dexos1 Gen 3 certified oil. The Duramax diesel takes Dexos D. Using the wrong spec oil can void your GM warranty.

Oil Specs by Engine

Before you buy anything, confirm your engine code. It's on your window sticker, your RPO label in the glove box, or in the driver-side door jamb. The spec table below covers model years 2019–2026.

Engine RPO Code Oil Spec Viscosity Capacity (w/ filter) Filter Part #
2.7L Turbo 4-cyl L3B Dexos1 Gen 3 0W-20 5.0 qts ACDelco PF64
5.3L V8 EcoTec3 L84 / L82 Dexos1 Gen 3 0W-20 8.0 qts ACDelco PF66
6.2L V8 EcoTec3 L87 Dexos1 Gen 3 0W-20 8.0 qts ACDelco PF66
3.0L Duramax Diesel LM2 / LZ0 Dexos D 0W-20 7.4 qts ACDelco PF64E
Duramax Owners — Read This

The 3.0L Duramax requires Dexos D — not Dexos1. These are different specifications. Dexos D is a diesel-specific certification. Using standard Dexos1 oil in a Duramax can cause soot buildup and damage the DPF (diesel particulate filter). Make sure your oil jug says "Dexos D" before pouring.

What You'll Need

Gather your supplies before getting under the truck. Nothing worse than being halfway through a drain with no funnel in sight.

  • Correct oil (quantity per spec table above)
  • ACDelco filter (PF64 or PF66 per engine)
  • Oil drain pan (6+ quart capacity)
  • 15mm or 13mm socket for drain plug (depending on year — confirm yours)
  • Oil filter wrench (3/4" drive recommended for V8s)
  • Drive-on ramps or floor jack with jack stands
  • Nitrile gloves, shop rags
  • Torque wrench

Drain Plug Torque Specs

GM doesn't give you much margin for error on drain plugs. Over-tighten and you strip the oil pan threads — a much worse problem than an oil change. Under-tighten and you're leaving a trail down the highway. Use a torque wrench.

Engine Drain Plug Torque Plug Size Notes
2.7L Turbo (L3B) 18 lb-ft 13mm Replace plug gasket every 3 oil changes
5.3L V8 (L84) 18 lb-ft 15mm Standard aluminum pan — do not over-torque
6.2L V8 (L87) 18 lb-ft 15mm Same pan as 5.3L
3.0L Duramax (LM2) 26 lb-ft 17mm Steel pan — slightly higher torque spec

Step-by-Step Oil Change

These steps apply to all Silverado engines. The only things that change are the oil quantity, filter part number, and drain plug torque spec (see tables above).

  1. Warm up the engine 2–3 minutes — warm oil drains faster and carries more contaminants out with it. Don't run it hot or you'll burn yourself.
  2. Raise and support the truck safely — drive up on ramps, or jack it up and place it on jack stands. Never work under a truck supported only by a floor jack.
  3. Locate and loosen the drain plug — it's on the bottom of the oil pan. Position your drain pan under it before you pull the plug. The oil will shoot sideways initially.
  4. Pull the plug and let it drain completely — 5 minutes minimum. Inspect the plug and gasket for damage while you wait.
  5. Remove the old filter — use an oil filter wrench for extra grip. Have rags ready; some oil will spill when it comes loose. On V8s the filter is on the passenger side near the front of the block.
  6. Install the new filter — lightly oil the new filter's gasket with fresh oil. Thread it on by hand until the gasket seats, then an additional 3/4 turn. Do not use a wrench to tighten — hand tight is correct.
  7. Reinstall the drain plug — thread it in by hand first to avoid cross-threading, then torque to spec (see table above).
  8. Add fresh oil through the top — use the capacity listed in the spec table. Start with 1 quart less, then check the dipstick and top up.
  9. Run the engine 30 seconds, shut off, wait 5 minutes — check for leaks at the drain plug and filter, then re-check the dipstick. Top up if needed to the "Full" line.
  10. Reset the oil life monitor — see section below.
Crystal's Note

The V8 Silverados hold 8 quarts — that's two full 5-qt jugs minus 2 quarts. Buy two jugs, pour all of the first one in, then add from the second while checking the dipstick. You'll have leftover oil for next time. Don't pour all 8 quarts in blindly — overfilling damages seals and can hydro-lock a cylinder on startup.

Resetting the Oil Life Monitor

The Silverado's DIC (Driver Information Center) tracks oil life based on engine conditions, not just mileage. It needs to be manually reset after every oil change. The method varies slightly by generation but follows the same logic.

2019–2026 Silverado (T1 Platform)

  1. Press the Home button on the steering wheel to bring up the main DIC menu.
  2. Navigate to "Vehicle Information" using the up/down arrows on the steering wheel.
  3. Find "Oil Life" — it will show the current remaining percentage.
  4. Press and hold the checkmark/OK button for 3–5 seconds until you see "Oil Life Reset Confirmed" or the percentage resets to 100%.
Alternative Method

Turn ignition to ON (engine off). Press the accelerator pedal fully to the floor and release it three times within five seconds. The oil life indicator will flash and then reset. This works on most 2014–2026 Silverados if you can't navigate the DIC menus.

How Often Should You Change Your Oil?

GM's official guidance for the Silverado is "follow the oil life monitor." In practice, that typically works out to:

  • Normal driving: 7,500–10,000 miles with full synthetic Dexos1 Gen 3
  • Severe service (towing, short trips, dusty conditions): 5,000–7,500 miles
  • 3.0L Duramax diesel: Up to 7,500 miles with Dexos D — the diesel is actually very tolerant of extended intervals with the right oil

The old "every 3,000 miles" rule is a myth for modern full-synthetic oils. You're wasting money and oil if you're still doing that with a 2019+ Silverado.
